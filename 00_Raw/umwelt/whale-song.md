# Whale vocalization - Wikipedia
[Whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale) use a variety of sounds for [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_communication) and sensation. [1] The mechanisms used to produce sound vary from one family of

[cetaceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetaceans)to another.

[Marine mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal), including whales,

[dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolphin), and

[porpoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porpoise), are much more dependent on sound than land mammals due to the limited effectiveness of other senses in water.

[Sight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception)is less effective for marine mammals because of the way particulates in the ocean scatter

[light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light).

[Smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfaction)is also limited, as

[molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule)diffuse more slowly in water than in air, which makes smelling less effective. However, the

[speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound)is roughly three times greater in water than in the atmosphere at

[sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level). As sea mammals are so dependent on hearing to communicate and feed, environmentalists and

[cetologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetology)are concerned that they are being harmed by the increased

[ambient noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_noise)in the world's oceans caused by ships,

[sonar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonar)and marine

[seismic surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_seismology).


[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Melcón2012-2)The word "song" is used to describe the pattern of regular and predictable sounds made by some species of whales, especially the [humpback whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humpback_whale). This is included with or in comparison with music, and male humpback whales have been described as "inveterate composers" of songs that are "'strikingly similar' to human musical traditions". [3] This position has been complicated by more recent research, however.

It has been suggested that humpback songs communicate male fitness to female whales,

The humpback whale produces a series of repetitious sounds at varying frequencies known as whale song. Marine biologist Philip Clapham describes the song as "probably the most complex in the animal kingdom."[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-12)

Male humpback whales perform these vocalizations often during the mating season, and so it was initially believed the purpose of songs is to aid mate selection. [13] However, no evidence was found that links these songs to reprosexuality.

[

*]*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)The songs follow a distinct hierarchical structure. The base units of the song (sometimes loosely called the "[notes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Note_(music))") are single uninterrupted emissions of sound that last up to a few seconds. These sounds vary in frequency from 20 Hz to upward of 24 kHz (the typical human range of hearing is 20 Hz to 20 kHz). The units may be [frequency-modulated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_modulation) (i.e., the pitch of the sound may go up or down or stay the same during the note) or [amplitude-modulated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude_modulation) (get louder or softer). However, the adjustment of bandwidth on a spectrogram representation of the song reveals the essentially [pulsed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_(signal_processing)) nature of the FM sounds.[

[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]

A collection of four or six units is known as a sub-[phrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase), lasting perhaps ten seconds (see also [phrase (music)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase_(music))). [13] A collection of two sub-phrases is a phrase. A whale will typically repeat the same phrase over and over for two to four minutes. This is known as a theme. A collection of themes is known as a song.

The whale song will last up to 30 or so minutes, and will be repeated over and over again over the course of hours or even days.

[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Frankel-13)This "

[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Frankel-13)[Russian doll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_doll)" hierarchy of sounds suggests a syntactic structure

that is more human-like in its complexity than other forms of animal communication like bird songs, which have only linear structure.

Another unit may get steadily louder. The pace of evolution of a whale's song also changes—some years the song may change quite rapidly, whereas in other years little variation may be recorded.

Whales from non-overlapping regions sing entirely different songs.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Frankel-13)

As the song evolves, it appears that old patterns are not revisited. [13] An analysis of 19 years of whale songs found that while general patterns in song could be spotted, the same combination never recurred.

[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-phys-25)It was previously thought that most baleen whales make sounds at about 15–20 [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz). [26] However, a team of

[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:2-26)[Humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humans) produce [voiced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)) sounds by passing air through the [larynx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larynx). Within the larynx, when the [vocal cords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_cords) are brought close together, the passing air will force them to alternately close and open, separating the continuous airstream into discrete pulses of air that are heard as a vibration. [28] This vibration is further modified by

[speech organs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech_organs)in the

[oral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouth)and

[nasal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_cavity)cavities, creating sounds which are used in

[human speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_voice).

Cetacean sound production differs markedly from this mechanism. The precise mechanism differs in the two suborders of cetaceans: the *Odontoceti* ([toothed whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothed_whales), including dolphins) and the *Mysticeti* ([baleen whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baleen_whale), including the largest whales such as the [blue whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_whale)).

[Odontocetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odontocetes) produce rapid bursts of high-frequency clicks that are thought to be primarily for [echolocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_echolocation). Specialized organs in an odontocete produce collections of clicks and buzzes at frequencies from 0.2 to 150 kHz to obtain sonic information about its environment. Lower frequencies are used for distance echolocation, due to the fact that shorter wavelengths do not travel as far as longer wavelengths underwater. Higher frequencies are more effective at shorter distances, and can reveal more detailed information about a target. Echoes from clicks convey not only the distance to the target, but also the size, shape, speed, and vector of its movement. Additionally, echolocation allows the odontocete to easily discern the difference between objects that are different in material composition, even if visually identical, by their different densities. Individuals also appear to be able to isolate their own echoes during pod feeding activity without interference from other pod members' echolocations.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dolphins.org-29)

Whistles are used for communication. Four- to six-month-old calves develop unique sounds that they use most frequently throughout their lives. Such "signature whistles" are distinctive to the individual and may serve as a form of identification among other odontocetes. [29] Though a large pod of dolphins will produce a wide range of different noises, very little is known about the meaning of the sound. Frankel quotes one researcher who says listening to a school of odontocetes is like listening to a group of children at a school playground.


[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Frankel-13)The multiple sounds odontocetes make are produced by passing air through a structure in the head called the **phonic lips**. [30] Biologically the structure is

[homologous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_(biology))to an upper lip located in the nasal cavity, but mechanistically the phonic lips act similarly to human

[vocal "cords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_cords)" (vocal folds), which in humans are located in the

[larynx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larynx). As the air passes through this narrow passage, the phonic lip membranes are sucked together, causing the surrounding tissue to vibrate. These vibrations can, as with the vibrations in the human larynx, be consciously controlled with great sensitivity.

The vibrations pass through the tissue of the head to the

[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:0-30)[melon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melon_(whale)), which shapes and directs the sound into a beam of sound useful in echolocation. Every toothed whale except the

[sperm whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_whale)has two sets of phonic lips and is thus capable of making two sounds independently.

Once the air has passed the phonic lips it enters the

[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-31)[vestibular sac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vestibular_system). From there, the air may be recycled back into the lower part of the nasal complex, ready to be used for sound creation again, or passed out through the blowhole.


[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-32)The [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language) name for phonic lips, *museau de singe*, translates literally as "monkey's muzzle", which the phonic lip structure is supposed to resemble in sperm whales. [33] New cranial analysis using

[computed axial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computed_axial_tomography)and

[single photon emission computed tomography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_photon_emission_computed_tomography)scans in 2004 showed, at least in the case of

[bottlenose dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin), that air might be supplied to the nasal complex from the lungs, enabling the sound creation process to continue for as long as the dolphin can add air from the lungs.


[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-34)The sperm whale's vocalizations are all based on clicking, described in four types: the usual echolocation, creaks, codas, and slow clicks. [35] The most distinctive vocalizations are codas, which are short rhythmic sequences of clicks, mostly numbering 3–12 clicks, in stereotyped patterns.


[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead-2024-36)They are the result of

[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead_book-35)[vocal learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_learning)within a stable social group.

Some codas express clan identity, and denote different patterns of travel, foraging, and socializing or avoidance among clans.

[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gero-2016-37)

[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Safina-2020-pp16–19-38)As "arbitrary traits that function as reliable indicators of cultural group membership," clan identity codas act as symbolic markers that modulate interactions between individuals.

[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-39)

[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hersh-2022-40)Individual identity in sperm whale vocalizations is an ongoing scientific issue, however. A distinction needs to be made between cues and signals. Human acoustic tools can distinguish individual whales by analyzing micro-characteristics of their vocalizations, and the whales can probably do the same. This does not prove that the whales deliberately use some vocalizations to signal individual identity in the manner of the [signature whistles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signature_whistle) that bottlenose dolphins use as individual labels.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gero-2016-37)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hersh-2022-40)

[Mysticetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baleen_whale) do not have phonic lip structure. Instead, they have a larynx that appears to play a role in sound production, as it has vocal folds (vocal "cord") homologs in the U-shaped fold supported by arytenoid cartilages. [41] Whales do not have to exhale in order to produce sound, as they capture the air in a laryngeal sac. It is likely that they recycle air from this sac back to the lungs for the next vocalization.

They do not have bony cranial sinuses, but there is a pterygoid air sac. Its role in sound production is unclear (perhaps resonance?), but most likely it is for hearing, as it appears to preserve an airspace at depth around the ear ossicles.
