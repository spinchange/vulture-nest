*Cercopithecus campbelli*[Lowe's mona monkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowe%27s_mona_monkey)**Campbell's mona monkey** (* Cercopithecus campbelli*), also known as

**Campbell's guenon**and

**Campbell's monkey**, is a species of

[primate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primate)in the family

[Cercopithecidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cercopithecidae)found in the

[Ivory Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivory_Coast),

[Gambia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gambia),

[Ghana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana),

[Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinea),

[Guinea-Bissau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinea-Bissau),

[Liberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberia),

[Senegal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senegal), and

[Sierra Leone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Leone).

It was named for

[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn_status_16_November_2021-2)[Henry Dundas Campbell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Dundas_Campbell), in 1838.


[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-3)[Lowe's mona monkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowe%27s_mona_monkey)was previously considered a subspecies of Campbell's mona monkey. The

[International Union for Conservation of Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature)has rated this species as being a near-threatened species because it has a wide range and is able to adapt to degraded habitats.


[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn_status_16_November_2021-2)Campbell's mona monkey is native to Senegal, Gambia, Guinea-Bissau, Guinea, Sierra Leone and Liberia as far east as the [Cavally River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavally_River) on the border with Ivory Coast, and also the island of [Caravela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caravela), off Guinea Bissau. Its habitat is lowland forest, both primary and secondary, gallery forest, mangrove swamps, agricultural land and scrubland.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn_status_16_November_2021-2)

Campbell's mona monkey is a sociable and territorial species, living in small groups of about eight individuals. Around dawn and dusk, the dominant male climbs to a perch on an emergent tree and issues a series of booms. The sound carries for at least a kilometre, and other males join in. This monkey often associates with monkeys of other species and engages in inter-species territorial calling which obey certain ritual rules. [4] This species has one of the more advanced forms of

[animal communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_communication), with a rudimentary syntax.
