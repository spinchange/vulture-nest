The **birds-of-paradise** are members of the [family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_(biology)) **Paradisaeidae** of the order [Passeriformes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passeriformes). They are found mainly in [New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Guinea), as well as eastern [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia) and the [Moluccas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moluccas). The family has 44 species in 17 [genera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genera). The members of this family are perhaps best known for the [plumage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plumage) of the males of the species, the majority of which are [sexually dimorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexually_dimorphic). The males of these species tend to have very long, elaborate feathers extending from the beak, wings, tail, or head. For the most part, they are confined to dense [rainforest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainforest) habitats. The diet of all species is dominated by fruit and to a lesser extent [arthropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthropods). The birds-of-paradise have a variety of breeding systems, ranging from [monogamy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals) to [lek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lek_(mating_arena))-type[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-2)[polygamy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_sexual_behaviour#Polygamy).

A number of species are [threatened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threatened) by hunting and [habitat loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_loss).

The family Paradisaeidae is introduced (as Paradiseidae) in 1825 with *Paradisaea* as the [type genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_genus) by the English naturalist [William Swainson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Swainson).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-3)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-7) [b] For many years the birds-of-paradise were treated as being closely related to the

[bowerbirds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowerbird). Today while both are treated as being part of the Australasian lineage

[Corvida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corvida), the two are now thought to be only distantly related. The closest evolutionary relatives of the birds-of-paradise are the crow and jay family

[Corvidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corvidae), the monarch flycatchers

[Monarchidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch_flycatcher), and the Australian mudnesters

[Struthideidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_mudnester).


[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)A 2009 study examining the [mitochondrial DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA) of all species to examine the relationships within the family and to its nearest relatives estimated that the family emerged 24 million years ago, earlier than previous estimates. The study identified five [clades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clade) within the family, and placed the split between the first clade, which contains the monogamous [manucodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manucode) and [paradise-crow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradise-crow), and all the other birds-of-paradise, to be 10 million years ago. The second clade includes the [parotias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parotia) and the [King of Saxony bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_of_Saxony_bird-of-paradise). The third clade provisionally contains several genera, including *Seleucidis*, the *Drepanornis* sicklebills, *Semioptera*, *Ptiloris,* and *Lophorina*, although some of these are questionable. The fourth clade includes the *Epimachus* sicklebills, *Paradigalla,* and the astrapias. The final clade includes the *Cicinnurus* and the *Paradisaea* birds-of-paradise.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-irestedt-12)

The exact limits of the family have been the subject of revision as well. The three species of [satinbird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satinbird) (the genera * Cnemophilus* and

*) were treated as a subfamily of the birds-of-paradise, Cnemophilinae. In spite of differences in the mouth, foot morphology, and nesting habits they remained in the family until a 2000 study moved them to a separate family closer to the berrypeckers and longbills (*

[Loboparadisea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loboparadisea)[Melanocharitidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanocharitidae)).

The same study found that the

[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Cracraft-13)[Macgregor's bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacGregor%27s_honeyeater)was actually a member of the large Australasian

[honeyeater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeyeater)family. In addition to these three species, a number of systematically enigmatic species and genera have been considered potential members of this family. The two species of

[melampitta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melampitta), also from New Guinea, have been linked with the birds-of-paradise,

but their relationships remain uncertain, more recently being linked with the Australian mudnesters.

[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-14)The

[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)[silktail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silktail)of

[Fiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiji)has been linked with the birds-of-paradise many times since its discovery, but never formally assigned to the family. Recent molecular evidence now places the species with the

[fantails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantail).

Hybrid birds-of-paradise may occur when individuals of different species, that look similar and have overlapping ranges, confuse each other for their own species and crossbreed.

When [Erwin Stresemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Stresemann) realised that hybridisation among birds-of-paradise might be an explanation as to why so many of the described species were so rare, he examined many controversial specimens and, during the 1920s and 1930s, published several papers on his hypothesis. Many of the species described in the late 19th and early 20th centuries are now generally considered to be hybrids, though some are still subject to dispute; their status is not likely to be settled definitely without genetic examination of museum specimens, which will come soon in summer 2021 in North America, South America, Africa, Europe, Asia, and Australia, and some birds in an aviary in [Central Park Zoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Park_Zoo).[

*]*[needs update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items)Birds-of-paradise are closely related to the [corvids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corvidae). Birds-of-paradise range in size from the [king bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_bird-of-paradise) at 50 g (1.8 oz) and 15 cm (5.9 in) to the [curl-crested manucode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl-crested_manucode) at 44 cm (17 in) and 430 g (15 oz). The male [black sicklebill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_sicklebill), with its long tail, is the longest species at 110 cm (43 in). In most species, the tails of the males are larger and longer than those of the females, the differences ranging from slight to extreme. The wings are rounded and, in some species, structurally modified on the males in order to make sound. There is considerable variation in the family with regard to [bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beak) shape. Bills may be long and decurved, as in the sicklebills and riflebirds, or small and slim like the [Astrapias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrapia). As with body size, bill size varies between the sexes, although species where the females have larger bills than the males are more common, particularly in the insect-eating species.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)

[Plumage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plumage) variation between the sexes is closely related to the breeding system. The manucodes and paradise-crow, which are socially monogamous, are [sexually monomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sex#Sexual_monomorphism). So are the two species of * Paradigalla*, which are polygamous. All these species have generally black plumage with varying amounts of green and blue

[iridescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridescence).

The female plumage of the dimorphic species is typically drab to blend in with their habitat, unlike the bright attractive colours found on the males. Younger males of these species have female-like plumage, and sexual maturity takes a long time, with the full adult plumage not being obtained for up to seven years. This affords the younger males protection from predators of more subdued colours and also reduces hostility from adult males.

[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)The majority of birds-of-paradise live in tropical forests, including [rainforests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainforest), swamps, and [moss forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moss_forest), [9] nearly all of them solitary tree dwellers.

Several species have been recorded in coastal

[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-honoluluzoo.org-17)[mangroves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mangrove).

The southernmost species, the

[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heads-18)[paradise riflebird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradise_riflebird)of

[Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia), lives in sub-tropical and temperate wet forests. As a group the

[manucodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manucode)are the most plastic in their habitat requirements; in particular, the

[glossy-mantled manucode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossy-mantled_manucode), which inhabits both forest and open savanna woodland.

Mid-montane habitats are the most commonly occupied habitat, with thirty of the forty species occurring in the 1,000–2,000 m (3,300–6,600 ft) altitudinal band.

[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)

[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heads-18)The diet of the birds-of-paradise is dominated by fruit and arthropods, although small amounts of nectar and small vertebrates may also be taken. The ratio of the two food types varies by species, with fruit predominating in some species, and arthropods dominating the diet in others. The ratio of the two will affect other aspects of the behaviour of the species; for example, [frugivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frugivorous) species tend to feed in the forest canopy, whereas [insectivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insectivore) may feed lower down in the middle storey. Frugivores are more social than the insectivores, which are more solitary and [territorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territory_(animal)).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)

Even the birds-of-paradise that are primarily insect eaters will still take large amounts of fruit. The family is overall an important seed disperser for the forests of New Guinea, as they do not digest the seeds. Species that feed on fruit will range widely searching for fruit, and while they may join other fruit-eating species at a fruiting tree, they will not associate with them otherwise and will not stay with other species for long. Fruit is eaten while perched and not in the air, and birds-of-paradise are able to use their feet as tools to manipulate and hold their food, allowing them to extract certain capsular fruit. There is some niche differentiation in fruit choice by species and any one species will only consume a limited number of fruit types compared to the large choice available. For example, the [trumpet manucode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trumpet_manucode) and [crinkle-collared manucode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crinkle-collared_manucode) will eat mostly figs, whereas the [Lawes's parotia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawes%27s_parotia) focuses mostly on berries and the [greater lophorina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_lophorina) and [Raggiana bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raggiana_bird-of-paradise) take mostly capsular fruit.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)

Most species have elaborate mating rituals, with at least eight species exhibiting [lek mating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lek_mating) systems, [17] including the genus

*Paradisaea*. Others, such as the

*and*

[Cicinnurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cicinnurus)*species, have highly ritualised mating dances. Across the family (Paradisaeidae), female preference is incredibly important in shaping the courtship behaviors of males and, in fact, drives the evolution of ornamental combinations of sound, color, and behavior.*

[Parotia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parotia)Males are

[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-BOP2018-20)[polygamous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_sexual_behaviour#Polygamy)in the

[sexually dimorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexually_dimorphic)species, but

[monogamous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals)in at least some of the monomorphic species.

[Hybridisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_(biology))is frequent in these birds, suggesting the polygamous species of bird of paradise are very closely related despite being in different genera. Many hybrids have been described as new species in the past,

and doubt remains regarding whether some forms, such as

[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-21)[Rothschild's lobe-billed bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rothschild%27s_lobe-billed_bird-of-paradise), are valid.


[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-22)Birds-of-paradise build their nests from soft materials, such as leaves, ferns, and vine tendrils, typically placed in a tree fork. [21] The typical number of eggs in each

[clutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clutch_(eggs))varies among the species and is not known for every species. For larger species, it is almost always just one egg, but smaller species may produce clutches of 2–3 eggs.

Eggs hatch after 16–22 days, and the young leave the nest at between 16 and 30 days of age.

[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-24)

[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-EoB-23)Societies of New Guinea often use bird-of-paradise plumes in their [dress and rituals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Papua_New_Guinea), and the plumes were popular in Europe in past centuries as adornment for ladies' [millinery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hat). Hunting for plumes and [habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction) have reduced some species to endangered status; habitat destruction due to [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation) is now the predominant threat.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HBW-11)

Best known are the members of the genus * Paradisaea*, including the

[type species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_species), the

[greater bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_bird-of-paradise),

*Paradisaea apoda*. This species was described from specimens brought back to Europe from trading expeditions in the early sixteenth century. These specimens had been prepared by native traders by removing their wings and feet so that they could be used as decorations. This was not known to the explorers, and in the absence of information, many beliefs arose about them. They were briefly thought to be the mythical

[phoenix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)). The often footless and wingless condition of the skins led to the belief that the birds never landed but were kept permanently aloft by their plumes. The first Europeans to encounter their skins were the voyagers in

[Ferdinand Magellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan)'s

[circumnavigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumnavigation)of the

[Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth).


[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-25)[Antonio Pigafetta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Pigafetta)wrote that "The people told us that those birds came from the terrestrial paradise, and they call them bolon diuata, that is to say, 'birds of God'."

This is the origin of both the name "bird of paradise" and the specific name

[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-26)*apoda*– without feet.

An alternate account by

[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-27)[Maximilianus Transylvanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilianus_Transylvanus)used the term Mamuco Diata, a variant of Manucodiata, which was used as a synonym for birds-of-paradise up to the 19th century.

In recent years the availability of pictures and videos about birds of paradise on the internet has raised the interest of birdwatchers around the world. Many of them fly to [West Papua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_New_Guinea) to watch various species of birds of paradise from [Wilson's bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilson%27s_bird-of-paradise) (*Diphyllodes respublica*) and [red bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_bird-of-paradise) (Paradisaea rubra) in [Raja Ampat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raja_Ampat) to [lesser bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesser_bird-of-paradise) (*Paradisaea minor*), [magnificent riflebird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnificent_riflebird) (*Ptiloris magnificus*), [king bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_bird-of-paradise) (*Cicinnurus regius*), [Vogelkop lophorina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vogelkop_lophorina) (*Lophorina niedda*), [26] and

[magnificent bird-of-paradise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnificent_bird-of-paradise)(

*Diphyllodes magnificus*) in

[Susnguakti](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Susnguakti&action=edit&redlink=1)forest.

This activity significantly reduces the number of local villagers who are involved in the hunting of paradise birds.[

[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]

Hunting of birds of paradise has occurred for a long time, possibly since the beginning of human settlement. [27] It is a peculiarity that among the most frequently hunted species, males start mating opportunistically even before they grow their ornamental plumage. This may be an adaptation to maintaining population levels in the face of hunting pressures, which have probably been present for hundreds of years.


[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-30)The naturalist, explorer, and author [Alfred Russel Wallace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace) spent six years in the region, which he chronicled in * The Malay Archipelago* (published in 1869). His expedition team shot, collected, and described many specimens of animals and birds, including the great, king, twelve-wired, superb, red, and six-shafted birds of paradise.


[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-31)[Hunting to provide plumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plume_hunting) for the millinery trade was extensive in the late 19th and early 20th century, [30] but today the birds have legal protection except for hunting at a sustainable level to fulfill the ceremonial needs of the local tribal population.
