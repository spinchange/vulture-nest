**Biosemiotics** (from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek) βίος *bios*, "life" and σημειωτικός *sēmeiōtikos*, "observant of signs") is a field of [semiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiotics) and [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) that studies the prelinguistic meaning-making, biological [interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_(logic)) processes, production of [signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(semiotics)) and [codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_(semiotics)) and [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) processes in the biological realm.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-DF-1)

Biosemiotics integrates the findings of biology and semiotics and proposes a [paradigmatic shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm_shift) in the scientific view of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life), in which [semiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiosis) (sign process, including [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_(semiotics)) and interpretation) is one of its immanent and intrinsic features. [2] The term

*biosemiotic*was first used by

[Friedrich S. Rothschild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_S._Rothschild)in 1962,

but

[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-3)[Thomas Sebeok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Sebeok),

[Thure von Uexküll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thure_von_Uexk%C3%BCll),

[Jesper Hoffmeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesper_Hoffmeyer)and many others have implemented the term and field.

The field is generally divided between theoretical and applied biosemiotics.

[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-4)Insights from biosemiotics have also been adopted in the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities) and [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_sciences), including [human–animal studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrozoology), human–plant studies[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-5) [6] and cybersemiotics.


[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-7)Biosemiotics is the study of meaning making processes in the living realm, or, to elaborate, a study of

[signification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(semiotics)),[communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication)and[habit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habituation)formation of[living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life)processes[semiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiosis)(creating and changing sign relations) in living nature- the biological basis of all signs and sign interpretation
- interpretative processes, codes and cognition in organisms

According to the basic types of semiosis under study, biosemiotics can be divided into

- vegetative semiotics (also
*endosemiotics*, or[phytosemiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytosemiotics)),the study of semiosis at the cellular and molecular level (including the translation processes related to genome and the organic form or phenotype);[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-8)vegetative semiosis occurs in all organisms at their cellular and tissue level; vegetative semiotics includes prokaryote semiotics, sign-mediated interactions in bacteria communities such as[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-9)[quorum sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quorum_sensing)and quorum quenching. [zoosemiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoosemiotics)or animal semiotics,or the study of animal forms of knowing;[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-10)animal semiosis occurs in the organisms with[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-11)[neuromuscular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuromuscular)system, also includes[anthroposemiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthroposemiotics), the study of semiotic behavior in humans.

According to the dominant aspect of semiosis under study, the following labels have been used: biopragmatics, biosemantics, and biosyntactics.

Apart from [Charles Sanders Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce) (1839–1914) and [Charles W. Morris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_W._Morris) (1903–1979), early pioneers of biosemiotics were [Jakob von Uexküll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_von_Uexk%C3%BCll) (1864–1944), [Heini Hediger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heini_Hediger) (1908–1992), [Giorgio Prodi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgio_Prodi) (1928–1987), [Marcel Florkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Florkin) (1900–1979) and [Friedrich S. Rothschild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_S._Rothschild) (1899–1995); the founding fathers of the contemporary interdiscipline were [Thomas Sebeok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Sebeok) (1920–2001) and [Thure von Uexküll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thure_von_Uexk%C3%BCll) (1908–2004).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-12)

In the 1980s a circle of mathematicians active in Theoretical Biology, [René Thom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Thom) ([Institut des Hautes Etudes Scientifiques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_Etudes_Scientifiques)), Yannick Kergosien ([Dalhousie University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalhousie_University) and [Institut des Hautes Etudes Scientifiques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_Etudes_Scientifiques)), and [Robert Rosen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Rosen_(theoretical_biologist)) ([Dalhousie University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalhousie_University), also a former member of the Buffalo group with [Howard H. Pattee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howard_H._Pattee)), explored the relations between Semiotics and Biology using such headings as "Nature Semiotics",[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-13) [14] "Semiophysics",

or "Anticipatory Systems"

[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-15)and taking a modeling approach.

In 2001, an annual international conference for biosemiotic research known as the *Gatherings in Biosemiotics* [18] was inaugurated, and has taken place every year since.

In 2004, a group of biosemioticians – [Marcello Barbieri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcello_Barbieri), [Claus Emmeche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_Emmeche), [Jesper Hoffmeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesper_Hoffmeyer), [Kalevi Kull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalevi_Kull), and Anton Markoš – decided to establish an international journal of biosemiotics. Under their editorship, the *Journal of Biosemiotics* was launched by [Nova Science Publishers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_Science_Publishers) in 2005 (two issues published), and with the same five co-editors * Biosemiotics* was launched by

[Springer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Springer_Science)in 2008. The book series

*Biosemiotics*(Springer), edited by Claus Emmeche, Donald Favareau, Kalevi Kull, and Alexei Sharov, began in 2007 and 27 volumes have been published in the series by 2024.

The [International Society for Biosemiotic Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Society_for_Biosemiotic_Studies) was established in 2005 by Donald Favareau and the five editors listed above. [19] A collective programmatic paper on the basic theses of biosemiotics appeared in 2009.

and in 2010, an 800 page textbook and anthology,

[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-20)*Essential Readings in Biosemiotics,*was published, with bibliographies and commentary by Donald Favareau.


[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-DF-1)One of roots for biosemiotics has been medical semiotics. In 2021 a book edited by Yogi Hendlin and Jonathan Hope appeared, *Food and Medicine: A Biosemiotic Perspective* (Springer Series in Biosemiotics), which addressed medical and health semiotics.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-21)

Since the work of [Jakob von Uexküll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_von_Uexk%C3%BCll) and [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger), several scholars in the humanities have engaged with or appropriated ideas from biosemiotics in their own projects; conversely, biosemioticians have critically engaged with or reformulated humanistic theories using ideas from biosemiotics and complexity theory. For instance, [Andreas Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Weber_(writer)) has reformulated some of [Hans Jonas's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Jonas) ideas using concepts from biosemiotics, [22] and biosemiotics have been used to interpret the poetry of

[John Burnside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Burnside).


[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-23)Since 2021, the American philosopher [Jason Josephson Storm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jason_Josephson_Storm) has drawn on biosemiotics and empirical research on [animal communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_communication) to propose *hylosemiotics*, a theory of ontology and communication that Storm believes could allow the humanities to move beyond the [linguistic turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistic_turn).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Storm-24)

[John Deely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Deely)'s work also represents an engagement between humanistic and biosemiotic approaches. Deely was trained as a historian and not a biologist but discussed biosemiotics and zoosemiotics extensively in his introductory works on semiotics and clarified terms that are relevant for biosemiotics. [25] Although his idea of

[physiosemiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Physiosemiotics&action=edit&redlink=1)was criticized by practicing biosemioticians, Paul Cobley, Donald Favareau, and Kalevi Kull wrote that "the debates on this conceptual point between Deely and the biosemiotics community were always civil and marked by a mutual admiration for the contributions of the other towards the advancement of our understanding of sign relations."
