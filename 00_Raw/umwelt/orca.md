The **orca** (* Orcinus orca*), or

**killer whale**, is a

[toothed whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothed_whale)and the largest member of the

[oceanic dolphin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanic_dolphin)family. The only

[extant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extant_taxon)species in the genus

*, it is recognizable by its distinct pigmentation; being mostly black on top, white on the bottom and having recognizable white eye patches. A*

[Orcinus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcinus)[cosmopolitan species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmopolitan_species), it inhabits a wide range of marine environments, from

[Arctic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Ocean)to

[Antarctic regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Ocean)to tropical seas, but is more commonly documented in temperate or cooler coastal waters. Scientists have proposed dividing the global population into

[races](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_(taxonomy)),

[subspecies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspecies), or possibly even

[species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species).

Orcas are [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) with a diverse diet. [Individual populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orca_types_and_populations) often specialize in particular types of prey, including [bony fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bony_fish), [sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shark), [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batoids), and [marine mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal) such as [seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinniped), [dolphins, and whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetacea). They are highly [social](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_animal), with some populations forming stable [matrilineal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrilineal) family groups (pods). Their sophisticated hunting techniques and vocal behaviors, often unique to specific groups and passed down from generation to generation, are considered to be manifestations of [animal culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_culture). The most studied populations are off the west coast of North America, which include fish-eating "residents", mammal-eating "transients", and offshores.

The [International Union for Conservation of Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature) (IUCN) lists the orca's [conservation status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_status) as [data deficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_deficient) as multiple orca types may represent [distinct species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_problem). Some local populations are [threatened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threatened_species) or [endangered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_species) due to prey depletion, [habitat loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_loss), pollution (by [PCBs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl)), captures for [marine parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal_park), and [conflicts with fisheries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_fishing#Environmental_Risk). In late 2005, the [southern resident orcas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_resident_killer_whale) were added to the [U.S. Endangered Species list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service_list_of_endangered_species_of_mammals_and_birds).

Orcas have been revered by indigenous peoples while Western cultures have historically feared them. They have been taken by whalers when stocks of larger species have declined. The orca's image took a positive turn in the 1960s, due to greater public and scientific awareness and their display in captivity. Since then, orcas have been trained to perform in marine parks, a practice that has been criticized as unethical. Orcas rarely pose a threat to humans, and no fatal attack has been recorded in the wild. However, [captive orcas have injured or killed their handlers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orca_attacks#Captive_orca_attacks) in marine theme parks.

Orcas are often referred to as "killer whales" because ancient sailors saw them hunt larger whales. [7] Since the 1960s, the term "orca" has increasingly replaced "killer whale" in common usage.

[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pitman_et_al._2010-31)## Characteristics

Orcas are the largest extant members of the dolphin family. Males typically range from 6 to 8 m (20 to 26 ft) long and weigh in excess of 6 t (5.9 long tons; 6.6 short tons). Females are smaller, generally ranging from 5 to 7 m (16 to 23 ft) and weighing about 3 to 4 t (3.0 to 3.9 long tons; 3.3 to 4.4 short tons). [32] Orcas may attain larger sizes as males have been recorded at 10 m (33 ft) and females at 8.5 m (28 ft).


[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)Large males can reach a weight of over 10 t (9.8 long tons; 11 short tons), large females can reach a weight of over 7.5 t (7.4 long tons; 8.3 short tons).

[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeptnerNasimovichBannikovHoffmann1996683-34)

[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-35)

[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-36)Calves at birth weigh about 180 kg (400 lb) and are about 2.4 m (7 ft 10 in) long.

[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-37)

[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-38)The skeleton of the orca is typical for an oceanic dolphin, but more robust.

[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-39)

[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)With their distinctive pigmentation, [33] adult orcas are rarely confused with any other species.

When seen from a distance, juveniles can be confused with

[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200120-40)[false killer whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_killer_whale)or

[Risso's dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risso%27s_dolphin).

The orca is mostly black but with sharply bordered white areas. The entire lower jaw is white and from here, the colouration stretches across the underside to the genital area; narrowing and expanding some, and extending into lateral flank patches close to the end. The tail fluke (fin) is also white on the underside, while the eyes have white oval-shaped patches behind and above them, and a grey or white "saddle patch" exists behind the dorsal fin and across the back.

[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-41)

[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)Males and females also have different patterns of black and white skin in their genital areas.

[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin2-42)In newborns, the white areas are yellow or orange coloured.

[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllisBalcomb200045-43)

[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)Antarctic orcas may have pale grey to nearly white backs.

[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin2-42)Some Antarctic orcas are brown and yellow due to

[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200120-40)[diatoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatoms)in the water.

Both

[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pitman2003-22)[albino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albino)and

[melanistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanistic)orcas have been documented.


[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)Orca [pectoral fins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pectoral_fin) are large and rounded, resembling paddles, with those of males significantly larger than those of females. [Dorsal fins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorsal_fin) also exhibit [sexual dimorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism), with those of males about 1.8 m (5 ft 11 in) high, more than twice the size of the female's, with the male's fin more like an elongated [isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle), whereas the female's is more curved.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-44) [45] In the skull, adult males have longer lower jaws than females, as well as larger

[occipital crests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/External_occipital_crest).

The snout is blunt and lacks the beak of other species.

[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeptnerNasimovichBannikovHoffmann1996681-46)The orca's teeth are very strong, and its jaws exert a powerful grip; the upper teeth fall into the gaps between the lower teeth when the mouth is closed. The firm middle and back teeth hold prey in place, while the front teeth are inclined slightly forward and outward to protect them from powerful jerking movements.

[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Heyning1988-33)

[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeptnerNasimovichBannikovHoffmann1996683-34)Orcas have good eyesight above and below the water, excellent hearing, and a good sense of touch. They have exceptionally sophisticated [echolocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_echolocation) abilities, detecting the location and characteristics of prey and other objects in the water by emitting clicks and listening for echoes, [47] as do other members of the dolphin family. The mean body temperature of the orca is 36 to 38 °C (97 to 100 °F).


[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-seaworld1-48)Like most marine mammals, orcas have a layer of insulating

[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-49)[blubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blubber)ranging from 7.6 to 10 cm (3.0 to 3.9 in) thick beneath the skin.

The pulse is about 60 heartbeats per minute when the orca is at the surface, dropping to 30 beats/min when submerged.

[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-seaworld1-48)

[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Spencer,_Gornall_&_Poulter_(1967)-50)An individual orca can often be identified from its dorsal fin and saddle patch. Variations such as nicks, scratches, and tears on the dorsal fin and the pattern of white or grey in the saddle patch are unique. Published directories contain identifying photographs and names for hundreds of North Pacific animals. Photographic identification has enabled the local population of orcas to be counted each year rather than estimated, and has enabled great insight into life cycles and social structures.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObeeEllis19921–27-51)

## Range and habitat

Orcas are found in all oceans and most seas. Due to their [enormous range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution), numbers, and density, relative distribution is difficult to estimate, [52] but they clearly prefer higher latitudes and coastal areas over

[pelagic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pelagic_zone)environments.

Areas which serve as major study sites for the species include the coasts of

[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200121-53)[Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland), Norway, the

[Valdés Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vald%C3%A9s_Peninsula)of Argentina, the

[Crozet Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crozet_Islands), New Zealand and parts of the west coast of North America, from

[California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California)to

[Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska).

Systematic surveys indicate the highest densities of orcas (>0.40 individuals per 100 km

[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird2002128-54)2) in the northeast Atlantic around the

[Norwegian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway)coast, in the north Pacific along the

[Aleutian Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleutian_Islands), the

[Gulf of Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska)and in the

[Southern Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Ocean)off much of the coast of

[Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica). They are considered "common" (0.20–0.40 individuals per 100 km

2) in the eastern Pacific along the coasts of

[British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Columbia),

[Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_(state))and

[Oregon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oregon), in the North Atlantic Ocean around Iceland and the

[Faroe Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faroe_Islands).


[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Forney2007-52)In the Antarctic, orcas range up to the edge of the [pack ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pack_ice) and are believed to venture into the denser pack ice, finding open [leads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_(sea_ice)) much like [beluga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beluga_(whale)) whales in the Arctic. However, orcas are merely seasonal visitors to Arctic waters, and do not approach the pack ice in the summer. With the rapid [Arctic sea ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice) decline in the [Hudson Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hudson_Strait), their range now extends deep into the northwest Atlantic. [55] Occasionally, orcas swim into freshwater rivers. They have been documented 100 mi (160 km) up the

[Columbia River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_River)in the United States.


[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200210-56)They have also been found in the

[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-57)[Fraser River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraser_River)in Canada and the

[Horikawa River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horikawa_River)in Japan.


[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200210-56)Migration patterns are poorly understood. Each summer, the same individuals appear off the coasts of British Columbia and Washington. Despite decades of research, where these animals go for the rest of the year remains unknown. Transient pods have been sighted from southern Alaska to central California.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200524–29-58)

### Population

Worldwide population estimates are uncertain, but recent consensus suggests a minimum of 50,000 (2006).[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllis2006-59)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn_status_19_November_2021-4) [60] Local estimates include roughly 25,000 in the Antarctic, 8,500 in the tropical Pacific, 2,250–2,700 off the cooler northeast Pacific and 500–1,500 off Norway.

Japan's Fisheries Agency estimated in the 2000s that 2,321 orcas were in the seas around Japan.

[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-63)## Feeding

Orcas are [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator), meaning that they themselves have no natural predators. They are sometimes called "wolves of the sea", because they hunt in groups like wolf packs. [64] Orcas hunt varied prey including fish,

[cephalopods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalopod), mammals,

[seabirds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seabird), and

[sea turtles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_turtles).

Different populations or ecotypes may specialize, and some can have a dramatic impact on prey species.

[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200517-65)However, whales in tropical areas appear to have more generalized diets due to lower food productivity.

[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-66)

[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Baird2006-67)Orcas spend most of their time at shallow depths,

[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Wier-68)but occasionally dive several hundred metres depending on their prey.

[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-69)

[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-70)On average, an orca eats 227 kilograms (500 lb) each day.

[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-72)### Fish

Fish-eating orcas prey on around 30 species of fish. Some populations in the [Norwegian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway) and [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland) sea specialize in [herring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herring) and follow that fish's autumnal migration to the Norwegian coast. [Salmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmon) account for 96% of northeast Pacific residents' diet, particularly [Chinook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_salmon) salmon which make up 65% of the salmon eaten by orcas.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200518-73)[Chum salmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chum_salmon) are also eaten, but smaller [sockeye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sockeye_salmon) and [pink salmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pink_salmon) are not a significant food item. Depletion of specific prey species in an area is, therefore, cause for concern for local populations, despite the high diversity of prey. [59] While salmon are usually hunted by an individual whale or a small group, herring are often caught using

[carousel feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carousel_feeding): the orcas force the herring into a tight ball by releasing bursts of bubbles or flashing their white undersides. They then slap the ball with their tail flukes, stunning or killing up to 15 fish at a time, then eating them one by one. Carousel feeding has been documented only in the Norwegian orca population, as well as some oceanic dolphin species.


[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-74)In New Zealand, [sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shark) and [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batoidea) appear to be important prey, including [eagle rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand_eagle_ray), [long-tail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorntail_stingray) and [short-tail stingrays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-tail_stingray), [common threshers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_thresher), [smooth hammerheads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_hammerhead), [blue sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_shark), [basking sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basking_sharks), and [shortfin makos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shortfin_mako).[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-orcashark-75) [76] Orcas pursuing sharks may herd them to the surface and strike them with their tail flukes,

while bottom-dwelling rays are cornered, pinned to the ground and taken to the surface.

[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-orcashark-75)In other parts of the world, orcas have preyed on

[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-77)[broadnose sevengill sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broadnose_sevengill_shark),


[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Engelbrecht-78)[whale sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_shark),


[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-79)and even

[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-80)[great white sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_white_shark), whose livers orcas have been observed to target by flipping the sharks onto their backs, inducing a paralysis called tonic immobility.


[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Engelbrecht-78)

[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pyle-81)Competition between orcas and white sharks is probable in regions where their diets overlap.

[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-82)The arrival of orcas in an area can cause white sharks to flee and forage elsewhere.

[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-85)### Mammals and birds

Orcas are sophisticated and effective predators of [marine mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal). They are recorded to prey on other cetacean species, usually smaller dolphins and [porpoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porpoise) such as [common dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_dolphin), [bottlenose dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin), [Pacific white-sided dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_white-sided_dolphin), [dusky dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dusky_dolphin), [harbour porpoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbour_porpoise) and [Dall's porpoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dall%27s_porpoise).[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin-86) [42] While hunting these species, orcas usually have to chase them to exhaustion. For highly social species, orca pods try to separate an individual from its group. Larger groups have a better chance of preventing their prey from escaping, which is killed by being thrown around, rammed and jumped on. Arctic orcas may attack

[beluga whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beluga_whale)and

[narwhals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narwhal)stuck in pools enclosed by sea ice, the former are also driven into shallower water where juveniles are grabbed.

By contrast, orcas appear to be wary of

[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin-86)[pilot whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilot_whale), which have been recorded to

[mob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobbing_(animal_behavior))and chase them.

Nevertheless, possible predation on

[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-87)[long-finned pilot whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-finned_pilot_whales)has been recorded in

[Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland), and one study suggests

[short-finned pilot whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-finned_pilot_whales)are among Caribbean orcas' prey.


[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-88)Killer whales have been recorded attacking short-finned pilot whales in

[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-89)[Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peru)as well.


[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-90)Orcas also prey on larger species such as [sperm whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_whale), [grey whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grey_whale), [humpback whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humpback_whale) and [minke whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minke_whales).[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin-86) [42] On three occasions in 2019 orcas were recorded to have killed

[blue whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_whale)off the south coast of Western Australia, among them was an individual measuring 18–22-meter (59–72 ft).

Large whales require much effort and coordination to kill and orcas often target calves. A hunt begins with a chase followed by a violent attack on the exhausted prey. Large whales often show signs of orca attack via tooth rake marks.

[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-91)Pods of female sperm whales sometimes protect themselves by forming a protective circle around their calves with their flukes facing outwards, using them to repel the attackers.

[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin-86)There is also evidence that humpback whales will defend against or mob orcas who are attacking either humpback calves or juveniles as well as members of other species.

[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-92)

[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-mobbing-93)[Prior to the advent of industrial whaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_whaling), great whales may have been the major food source for orcas. The introduction of modern whaling techniques may have aided orcas by the sound of [exploding harpoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpoon_cannon) indicating the availability of prey to scavenge, and compressed air inflation of whale carcasses causing them to float, thus exposing them to scavenging. However, the devastation of great whale populations by unfettered whaling has possibly reduced their availability for orcas, and caused them to expand their consumption of smaller marine mammals, thus contributing to the decline of these as well.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-EMM2009-94)

Other marine mammal prey includes [seal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinniped) species such as [harbour seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbour_seal), [elephant seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elephant_seal), [California sea lions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_sea_lion), [Steller sea lions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steller_sea_lion), [South American sea lions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_American_sea_lion) and [walruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walrus).[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Perrin-86) [42] Often, to avoid injury, orcas disable their prey before killing and eating it. This may involve throwing it in the air, slapping it with their tails, ramming it, or breaching and landing on it.

In steeply banked beaches off

[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeimlichBoran200145-95)[Península Valdés](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pen%C3%ADnsula_Vald%C3%A9s), Argentina, and the

[Crozet Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crozet_Islands), orcas feed on South American sea lions and

[southern elephant seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_elephant_seal)in shallow water, even

[beaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beached_whale)temporarily to grab prey before wriggling back to the sea. Beaching, usually fatal to cetaceans, is not an instinctive behaviour, and can require years of practice for the young.

Orcas can then release the animal near juvenile whales, allowing the younger whales to practice the difficult capture technique on the now-weakened prey.

[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200129-96)

[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeimlichBoran200145-95)In the Antarctic, type B orcas hunt

[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200261–62-97)[Weddell seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weddell_seal)and other prey by "wave-hunting". They

["spy-hop"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetacean_surfacing_behaviour#Spyhopping)to locate them resting on ice floes, and then swim in groups to create waves that wash over the floe. This washes the prey into the water, where other orcas lie in wait.


[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-98)

[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-99)In the [Aleutian Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleutian_Islands), a decline in [sea otter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_otter) populations in the 1990s was controversially attributed by some scientists to orca predation, although with no direct evidence. [100] The decline of sea otters followed a decline in seal populations,


[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-102)which in turn may be substitutes for their original prey, now decimated by industrial whaling.

[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-103)

[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-104)

[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-105)Orcas have been observed preying on

[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-106)[terrestrial mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_animal), such as

[moose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moose)swimming between islands off the northwest coast of North America.


[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200223-101)Orca

[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-BairdBaird2006-107)[cannibalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannibalism_(zoology))has also been reported based on analysis of stomach contents, but this is likely to be the result of scavenging remains dumped by whalers.

One orca was also attacked by its companions after being shot.

[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird2002124-108)However, in a 2026 study, evidence was found that Transient (Bigg's) orca cannibalizes Resident orca individuals, and it was also considered more appropriate to interpret the interactions between ecotypes as interspecies predation rather than cannibalism from a biological and behavioral perspective.

[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-114)## Behaviour

Day-to-day orca behaviour generally consists of [foraging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foraging), travelling, resting and socializing. Orcas frequently engage in [surface behaviour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_surfacing_behavior) such as breaching (jumping completely out of the water) and tail-slapping. These activities may have a variety of purposes, such as courtship, communication, dislodging [parasites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasite), or [play](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Play_(animal_behavior)). [Spyhopping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spyhopping) is a behaviour in which a whale holds its head above water to view its surroundings. [114] They can also reach speeds of up to 15.5 m/s (56 km/h; 35 mph).


[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-116)Resident orcas swim alongside

[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-117)[porpoises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porpoise)and other

[dolphins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolphin).


[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-OCR_11-2019-118)Orcas will engage in [surplus killing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surplus_killing), that is, killing that is not designed to be for food. As an example, a BBC film crew witnessed orcas in [British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Columbia) playing with a male [Steller sea lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steller_sea_lion) to exhaustion, but not eating it.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-119)

Some orcas have been observed swimming with dead [salmon on their heads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmon_hat), resembling hats.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-120)

### Social structure

Orcas have complex societies. Only [elephants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elephant) and [higher primates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_primate) live in comparably complex [social structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_structure). [120] Due to orcas' complex social bonds, many marine experts have concerns about how humane it is to keep

[them in captivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captive_killer_whales).


[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-122)Resident orcas in the eastern North Pacific live in particularly complex and stable social groups. Unlike any other known mammal social structure, resident whales live with their mothers for their entire lives. These family groups are based on [matrilines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matriline) consisting of the eldest female (matriarch) and her sons and daughters, and the descendants of her daughters, etc. The average size of a matriline is 5.5 animals. Because females can reach age 90, as many as four generations travel together. [45] These matrilineal groups are highly stable. Individuals separate for only a few hours at a time, to mate or forage. The permanent separation of an individual from a resident matriline has only been recorded once, in the case of an orca named

[Luna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luna_(orca)).


[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200512-123)Closely related matrilines form loose aggregations called pods, usually consisting of one to four matrilines. Unlike matrilines, pods may separate for weeks or months at a time.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200512-123)[DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) testing indicates resident males nearly always mate with females from other pods. [123] Clans, the next level of resident social structure, are composed of pods with similar dialects, and common but older maternal heritage. Clan ranges overlap, mingling pods from different clans.

The highest association layer is the community, which consists of pods that regularly associate with each other but share no maternal relations or dialects.

[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200512-123)

[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-125)Transient pods are smaller than resident pods, typically consisting of an adult female and one or two of her offspring. Males typically maintain stronger relationships with their mothers than other females. These bonds can extend well into adulthood. Unlike residents, extended or permanent separation of transient offspring from natal matrilines is common, with juveniles and adults of both sexes participating. Some males become "rovers" and do not form long-term associations, occasionally joining groups that contain reproductive females. [125] As in resident clans, transient community members share an acoustic repertoire, although regional differences in vocalizations have been noted.


[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200514-127)As with residents and transients, the lifestyle of these whales appears to reflect their diet; fish-eating orcas off Norway have resident-like social structures, while mammal-eating orcas in Argentina and the [Crozet Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crozet_Islands) behave more like transients.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllisBalcomb200027-128)

Orcas of the same sex and age group may engage in physical contact and synchronous surfacing. These behaviours do not occur randomly among individuals in a pod, providing evidence of "friendships".[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-129)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-130)

### Vocalizations

Multimedia relating to the orca
|

Like all [cetaceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetaceans), orcas depend heavily on underwater sound for orientation, feeding, and communication. They produce three categories of sounds: clicks, whistles, and pulsed calls. Clicks are believed to be used primarily for navigation and discriminating prey and other objects in the surrounding environment, but are also commonly heard during social interactions.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-noaa-60)

Northeast Pacific resident groups tend to be much more vocal than transient groups in the same waters. [130] Residents feed primarily on

[Chinook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_salmon)and

[chum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chum_salmon)salmon, which are insensitive to orca calls (inferred from the audiogram of Atlantic salmon). In contrast, the

[marine mammal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal)prey of transients hear whale calls well and thus transients are typically silent.

Vocal behaviour in these whales is mainly limited to surfacing activities and milling (slow swimming with no apparent direction) after a kill.

[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200520-131)

[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-132)All members of a resident pod use similar calls, known collectively as a [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect). Dialects are composed of specific numbers and types of discrete, repetitive calls. They are complex and stable over time. [132] Call patterns and structure are distinctive within matrilines.

Newborns produce calls similar to their mothers, but have a more limited repertoire.

[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-vocal-134)Individuals likely learn their dialect through contact with pod members.

[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200514-127)Family-specific calls have been observed more frequently in the days following a calf's birth, which may help the calf learn them.

[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-135)Dialects are probably an important means of maintaining group identity and cohesiveness. Similarity in dialects likely reflects the degree of relatedness between pods, with variation growing over time.

[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-136)When pods meet, dominant call types decrease and subset call types increase. The use of both call types is called biphonation. The increased subset call types may be the distinguishing factor between pods and inter-pod relations.

[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200515–16-137)

[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-vocal-134)Dialects also distinguish types. Resident dialects contain seven to 17 (mean = 11) distinctive call types. All members of the North American west coast transient community express the same basic dialect, although minor regional variation in call types is evident. Preliminary research indicates offshore orcas have group-specific dialects unlike those of residents and transients.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200515–16-137)

Norwegian and Icelandic [herring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herring)-eating orcas appear to have different vocalizations for activities like hunting. [137] A population that live in

[McMurdo Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McMurdo_Sound),

[Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica)have 28 complex burst-pulse and whistle calls.


[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-139)### Intelligence

Orcas have the second-heaviest brains among marine mammals [139] (after

[sperm whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_whale), which have the largest brain of any animal).

Orcas have more

[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-141)[gray matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grey_matter)and more cortical neurons than any mammal, including humans.

They can be

[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-142)[trained](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_training)in captivity and are often described as intelligent,


[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-cbs2010-143)although defining and measuring "intelligence" is difficult in a species whose environment and behavioural strategies are very different from those of humans.

[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200167-144)Orcas imitate others, and seem to deliberately teach skills to their kin. Off the

[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200167-144)[Crozet Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crozet_Islands), mothers push their calves onto the beach, waiting to pull the youngster back if needed.


[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeimlichBoran200145-95)In March 2023, a female orca was spotted with a newborn

[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200261–62-97)[pilot whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilot_whale)in

[Snæfellsnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sn%C3%A6fellsnes).


[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-145)People who have interacted closely with orcas offer numerous [anecdotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anecdotal_evidence) demonstrating the whales' curiosity, playfulness, and ability to solve problems. Alaskan orcas have not only learned how to steal fish from [longlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longline_fishing), but have also overcome a variety of techniques designed to stop them, such as the use of unbaited lines as decoys. [145] Once, fishermen placed their boats several miles apart, taking turns retrieving small amounts of their catch, in the hope that the whales would not have enough time to move between boats to steal the catch as it was being retrieved. The tactic worked initially, but the orcas adapted quickly and split into groups.


[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObeeEllis199242-146)In other anecdotes, researchers describe incidents in which wild orcas playfully tease humans by repeatedly moving objects the humans are trying to reach, [146] or suddenly start to toss around a chunk of ice after a human throws a snowball.


[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-148)In 2025, one of the [Salish Sea orcas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salish_Sea_orcas) was observed tearing off strands of bull kelp and rolling them around its body. This was the first observation of a killer whale using tools. Scientists called this behavior "allokelping."[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-149)

The orca's use of dialects and the passing of other learned behaviours from generation to generation have been described as a form of [animal culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_culture).[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-150)

According to one author:

"The complex and stable vocal and behavioural cultures of

[sympatric]groups of killer whales (Orcinus orca) appear to have no parallel outside humans and represent an independent evolution of cultural faculties."[[150]]

## Life cycle

Female orcas begin to mature at around the age of 10–13 and reach peak fertility around 20,[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-152) [152] experiencing periods of

[polyestrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyestrous)cycling separated by non-cycling periods of three to 16 months. Females can often breed until age 40, followed by a rapid decrease in fertility.

Orcas are among the few

[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Reproduction-153)[animals that undergo menopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menopause#Other_animals)and live for decades after they have finished breeding.


[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-154)The lifespans of wild females average 50 to 80 years.

[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-155)

[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Springer-45)Some are claimed to have lived substantially longer:

[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nationalgeographic.com-156)[Granny (J2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granny_(orca))was estimated by some researchers to have been as old as 105 years at the time of her death, though a biopsy sample indicated her age as 65 to 80 years.


[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200126-157)

[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-King-158)One of the oldest living orcas is Ocean Sun (identified as L25), a

[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Orcazine-159)[Southern Resident Orca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Resident_Orca), who is estimated to be more than 98 years old.


[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-160)It is thought that orcas held in captivity tend to have shorter lives than those in the wild, although this is subject to scientific debate.

[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nationalgeographic.com-156)

[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-robeck-162)

[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-163)Males mate with females from other pods, which prevents [inbreeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inbreeding). [Gestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestation_period) varies from 15 to 18 months.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200533-164) [45] Mothers usually calve a single offspring about once every five years. In resident pods, births occur at any time of year, although winter is the most common. Mortality is extremely high during the first seven months of life, when 37–50% of all calves die.


[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200535-165)[Weaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weaning)begins at about 12 months of age, and is complete by two years. According to observations in several regions, all male and female pod members participate in the care of the young.


[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeimlichBoran200135-121)Males sexually mature at the age of 15, but do not typically reproduce until age 21. Males can often reproduce until they are 59 years old. Wild males live around 29 to 30 years on average, with a maximum of about 50 to 60 years.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarwardine200126-157)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-166) [45] One male, known as

[Old Tom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Tom_(killer_whale)), was reportedly spotted every winter between the 1840s and 1930 off

[New South Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_South_Wales), Australia, which would have made him up to 90 years old. Examination of his teeth indicated he died around age 35,

but this method of age determination is now believed to be inaccurate for older animals.

[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-167)One male known to researchers in the

[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-168)[Pacific Northwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Northwest)(identified as J1) was estimated to have been 59 years old when he died in 2010.

The oldest male in Scottish waters is John Coe (identified as W001), estimated to be 65 years old,

[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-169)while a male in

[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-170)[Puget Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puget_Sound)named Harbeson (identified as T087) is thought to be around 64 years old.

Orcas are unique among cetaceans, as their caudal sections elongate with age, making their heads relatively shorter.

[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-171)

[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeptnerNasimovichBannikovHoffmann1996681-46)[Infanticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infanticide_(zoology)), once thought to occur only in captive orcas, was observed in wild populations by researchers off British Columbia on December 2, 2016. In this incident, an adult male killed the calf of a female within the same pod, with the adult male's mother also joining in the assault. It is theorized that the male killed the young calf in order to mate with its mother (something that [occurs in other carnivore species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infanticide_in_carnivores)), while the male's mother supported the breeding opportunity for her son. The attack ended when the calf's mother struck and injured the attacking male. Such behaviour matches that of many smaller dolphin species, such as the [bottlenose dolphin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin). [171] However, forced mating by males appears to be rare in killer whales.


[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Springer-45)## Conservation

In 2008, the [IUCN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN) (International Union for Conservation of Nature) changed its assessment of the orca's [conservation status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_status) from [conservation dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_dependent) to [data deficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_deficient), recognizing that one or more orca types may actually be separate, [endangered species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_species).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn_status_19_November_2021-4)[Depletion of prey species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overfishing), [pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_pollution), large-scale [oil spills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_spill), and [habitat disturbance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)) caused by noise and conflicts with boats are the most significant worldwide threats. [4] In January 2020, the first orca in England and Wales since 2001 was found dead with a large fragment of plastic in its stomach.


[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-173)Like other animals at the highest [trophic levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level), the orca is particularly at risk of poisoning from [bioaccumulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioaccumulation) of toxins, including [Polychlorinated biphenyls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl) (PCBs). [173] European

[harbour seals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbour_seal)have problems in reproductive and immune functions associated with high levels of PCBs and related contaminants, and a survey off the

[Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_(state))coast found PCB levels in orcas were higher than levels that had caused health problems in harbour seals.

Blubber samples in the

[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllisBalcomb200099-174)[Norwegian Arctic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Norway)show higher levels of PCBs, pesticides and

[brominated flame-retardants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brominated_flame-retardant)than in

[polar bears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_bear). A 2018 study published in

*found that global orca populations are poised to dramatically decline due to such toxic pollution.*

[Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_(journal))

[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-175)

[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-176)In the [Pacific Northwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Northwest), wild salmon stocks, a main resident food source, have declined dramatically in recent years. [4] In the

[Puget Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puget_Sound)region, only 75 whales remain with few births over the last few years.

On the west coast of Alaska and the

[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-177)[Aleutian Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleutian_Islands), seal and sea lion populations have also substantially declined.


[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllisBalcomb200098-178)In 2005, the United States government listed the [southern resident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_resident_killer_whales) community as an endangered population under the [Endangered Species Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act). [60] This community comprises three pods which live mostly in the

[Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Georgia)and

[Haro Straits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haro_Straits)and

[Puget Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puget_Sound)in

[British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Columbia)and Washington. They do not breed outside of their community, which was once estimated at 200 animals and later shrank to around 90.

In October 2008, the annual survey revealed seven were missing and presumed dead, reducing the count to 83.

[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lyke-179)This is potentially the largest decline in the population in the past 10 years. These deaths can be attributed to declines in

[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-researchers-180)[Chinook salmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_salmon).


[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-researchers-180)Scientist [Ken Balcomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Balcomb) has extensively studied orcas since 1976; he is the research biologist responsible for discovering [U.S. Navy sonar may harm orcas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammals_and_sonar#Naval_sonar-linked_incidents). He studied orcas from the Center for Whale Research, located in [Friday Harbor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friday_Harbor,_Washington), Washington. [180] He was also able to study orcas from "his home porch perched above Puget Sound, where the animals hunt and play in summer months".

In May 2003, Balcomb (along with other

[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pickrell-181)[whale watchers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_watching)near the Puget Sound coastline) noticed uncharacteristic behaviour displayed by the orcas. The whales seemed "agitated and were moving haphazardly, attempting to lift their heads free of the water" to escape the sound of the sonars.

"Balcomb confirmed at the time that strange underwater pinging noises detected with underwater microphones were sonar. The sound originated from a U.S. Navy frigate 12 miles (19 kilometres) distant, Balcomb said."

[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pickrell-181)The impact of sonar waves on orcas is potentially life-threatening. Three years prior to Balcomb's discovery, research in the Bahamas showed 14 beaked whales washed up on the shore. These whales were beached on the day U.S. Navy destroyers were activated into sonar exercise.

[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pickrell-181)Of the 14 whales beached, six of them died. These six dead whales were studied, and

[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pickrell-181)[CAT scans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT_scan)of two of the whale heads showed hemorrhaging around the brain and the ears, which is consistent with

[decompression sickness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decompression_sickness).


[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pickrell-181)Another conservation concern was made public in September 2008 when the Canadian government decided it was not necessary to enforce further protections (including the [Species at Risk Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_at_Risk_Act) in place to protect endangered animals along with their habitats) for orcas aside from the laws already in place. In response to this decision, six environmental groups sued the federal government, claiming orcas were facing threats along the [British Columbia Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Columbia_Coast) including increased boat traffic, water toxic wastes, and low salmon population, and the federal government did nothing to protect them.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-CBC-182)

[Underwater noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underwater_acoustics) from shipping, drilling, and other human activities is a significant concern in some key orca habitats, including [Johnstone Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnstone_Strait) and [Haro Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haro_Strait). [182] In the mid-1990s, loud underwater noises from

[salmon farms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmon_farm)were used to deter seals. Orcas also avoided the surrounding waters.

High-intensity sonar used by the

[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-184)[Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Navy)disturbs orcas along with other marine mammals.

Orcas are popular with

[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-185)[whale watchers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_watching), which may stress the whales and alter their behaviour, particularly if boats approach too closely or block their lines of travel.


[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-186)The [ Exxon Valdez oil spill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exxon_Valdez_oil_spill) adversely affected orcas in

[Prince William Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_William_Sound)and Alaska's

[Kenai Fjords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenai_Fjords)region. Eleven members (about half) of one resident pod disappeared in the following year. The spill damaged salmon and other prey populations, which in turn damaged local orcas. By 2009, scientists estimated the AT1 transient population (considered part of a larger population of 346 transients), numbered only seven individuals and had not reproduced since the spill. This population is expected to die out.

## Relationship with humans

### Indigenous cultures

The [Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Pacific_Northwest_Coast) feature orcas throughout [their art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northwest_Coast_art), history, spirituality and religion. The [Haida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haida_people) regarded orcas as the most powerful animals in the ocean, and their mythology tells of orcas living in houses and towns under the sea. According to these stories, they took on human form when submerged, and humans who drowned went to live with them. [188] For the

[Kwakwaka'wakw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kwakwaka%27wakw), the orca was regarded as the ruler of the undersea world, with

[sea lions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_lion)for slaves and dolphins for warriors.

In

[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrancisHewlett2007115–120-189)[Nuu-chah-nulth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuu-chah-nulth_people)and

[Kwakwaka'wakw mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kwakwaka%27wakw_mythology), orcas may embody the souls of deceased chiefs.

The

[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrancisHewlett2007115–120-189)[Tlingit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tlingit_people)of southeastern Alaska regarded the orca as custodian of the sea and a benefactor of humans.

The

[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFordEllisBalcomb200011-190)[Lummi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lummi_people)consider orca to be people, referring to them as "qwe'lhol'mechen" which means "our relations under the waves".


[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-191)The [Maritime Archaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_Archaic) people of [Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)) also had great respect for orcas, as evidenced by stone carvings found in a 4,000-year-old burial at the [Port au Choix Archaeological Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_au_Choix_Archaeological_Site).[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-192)[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-193)

In the tales and beliefs of the [Siberian Yupik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberian_Yupik) people, orcas are said to appear as [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf) in winter, and wolves as orcas in summer.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-rubow-194)[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-menow-195)[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ssipr-196) [196] Orcas are believed to assist their hunters in driving walrus.

Reverence is expressed in several forms: the boat represents the animal, as does a wooden carving hung from the hunter's belt.

[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-radio-198)Small

[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ssipr-196)[sacrifices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacrifice)such as tobacco or meat are strewn into the sea for them.


[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-radio-198)

[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-submit-197)The [Ainu people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ainu_people) of [Hokkaido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hokkaido), the [Kuril Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuril_Islands), and southern [Sakhalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sakhalin) often referred to orcas in their folklore and myth as * Repun Kamuy* (God of Sea/Offshore) to bring fortunes (whales) to the coasts, and there had been traditional funerals for stranded or deceased orcas akin to funerals for other animals such as

[brown bears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_bear).


[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-199)### Attacks by wild orcas on humans and animals

In [Western cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture), orcas were historically feared as dangerous, savage predators. [199] The first written description of an orca was given by

[Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder)

*circa*AD 70, who wrote, "Orcas (the appearance of which no image can express, other than an enormous mass of savage flesh with teeth) are the enemy of [other kinds of whale]... they charge and pierce them like warships ramming." (see citation in section

["Naming"](https://en.wikipedia.org#Naming), above).


[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-pliny1-201)Of the very few confirmed attacks on humans by wild orcas, none have been fatal. [201] In one instance, orcas tried to tip ice floes on which a dog team and

[photographer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Ponting)of the

[Terra Nova Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition)were standing.

The sled dogs' barking is speculated to have sounded enough like seal calls to trigger the orca's hunting curiosity. In the 1970s, a surfer in California was bitten, but the orca then retreated,

[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-203)and in 2005, a boy in Alaska who was splashing in a region frequented by harbour seals was bumped by an orca that apparently misidentified him as prey.

[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-204)

[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ketchikan-205)### Orca attacks on sailboats and small vessels

Beginning around 2020, one or more pods of orcas began to attack sailing vessels off the southern tip of Europe, and a few were sunk. At least 15 interactions between orcas and boats off the Iberian coast were reported in 2020. [205] According to the Atlantic Orca Working Group (GTOA) as many as 500 vessels have been damaged between 2020 and 2023.

In one video, an orca can be seen biting on one of the two rudders ripped from a catamaran near

[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-207)[Gibraltar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibraltar). The captain of the vessel reported this was the second attack on a vessel under his command and the orcas focused on the rudders. "Looks like they knew exactly what they are doing. They didn't touch anything else."

After an orca repeatedly rammed a vessel off the coast of Norway in 2023, there is a concern the behavior is spreading to other areas.

[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-208)This has led to recommendations that sailors now carry bags of sand.

[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-209)Dropping sand into the water near the rudder is thought to confuse the sonar signal.

[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-210)Experts were divided as to whether the behavior was some sort of revenge or protection response to a previous traumatic incident, or playful or frustrated attempts to get a boat's propeller to emit a stream of high-speed water.

[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-211)

[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-212)### Attacks on humans by captive orcas

Unlike wild orcas, captive orcas have made nearly two dozen [attacks on humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_whale_attacks_on_humans) since the 1970s, some of which have been fatal.[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-abc2006-213)[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-214)

### Human attacks on orcas

Competition with fishermen also led to orcas being regarded as pests. In the waters of the [Pacific Northwest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Northwest) and [Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland), the shooting of orcas was accepted and even encouraged by governments. [199] As an indication of the intensity of shooting that occurred until fairly recently, about 25% of the orcas captured in

[Puget Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puget_Sound)for aquariums through 1970 bore bullet scars.

The U.S. Navy claimed to have deliberately killed hundreds of orcas in Icelandic waters in 1956 with machine guns, rockets, and

[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200541-215)[depth charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth_charge).


[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-216)

[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-217)### Modern Western attitudes

Western attitudes towards orcas have changed dramatically in recent decades. In the mid-1960s and early 1970s, orcas came to much greater public and scientific awareness, starting with the live-capture and display of an orca known as [Moby Doll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moby_Doll), a [southern resident orca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_resident_orca) harpooned off [Saturna Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturna_Island) in 1964. [199] He was the first ever orca to be studied at close quarters alive, not postmortem.

[Moby Doll's impact in scientific research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moby_Doll%27s_impact_in_scientific_research)at the time, including the first scientific studies of an orca's sound production, led to two articles about him in the journal

*Zoologica*.


[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Schevill&Watkins-218)So little was known at the time, it was nearly two months before the whale's keepers discovered what food (fish) it was willing to eat. To the surprise of those who saw him, Moby Doll was a docile, non-aggressive whale who made no attempts to attack humans.

[[218]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Newman&McGeer-219)

[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrancisHewlett200758–59-220)Between 1964 and 1976, 50 orcas from the Pacific Northwest were captured for display in [aquaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquarium), and public interest in the animals grew. In the 1970s, research pioneered by [Michael Bigg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Bigg) led to the discovery of the species' complex social structure, its use of vocal communication, and its extraordinarily stable mother–offspring bonds. Through [photo-identification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_photo-identification) techniques, individuals were named and tracked over decades.[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaird200273–80-221)

Bigg's techniques also revealed the Pacific Northwest population was in the low hundreds rather than the thousands that had been previously assumed. [199] The southern resident community alone had lost 48 of its members to captivity; by 1976, only 80 remained.

In the Pacific Northwest, the species that had unthinkingly been targeted became a cultural icon within a few decades.

[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeimlichBoran200111-222)

[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lyke-179)The public's growing appreciation also led to growing opposition to whale–keeping in aquariua. Only one whale has been taken in North American waters since 1976. In recent years, the extent of the public's interest in orcas has manifested itself in several high-profile efforts surrounding individuals. Following the success of the 1993 film * Free Willy*, the movie's captive star

[Keiko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keiko_(whale))was returned to the coast of his native

[Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland)in 2002. The director of the International Marine Mammal Project for the

[Earth Island Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_Island_Institute), David Phillips, led the efforts to return Keiko to the Iceland waters.

Keiko however did not adapt to the harsh climate of the

[[222]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Wood-223)[Arctic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Ocean), and died a year into his release after contracting

[pneumonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumonia_(non-human)), at the age of 27.

In 2002, the orphan

[[223]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-224)[Springer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Springer_(orca))was discovered in

[Puget Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puget_Sound), Washington. She became the first whale to be successfully reintegrated into a wild pod after human intervention, crystallizing decades of research into the vocal behaviour and social structure of the region's orcas.

The saving of Springer raised hopes that another young orca named

[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-autogenerated2-225)[Luna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luna_(killer_whale)), which had become separated from his pod, could be returned to it. However, his case was marked by controversy about whether and how to intervene, and in 2006, Luna was killed by a boat propeller.


[[225]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-226)### Whaling

The earliest known records of commercial hunting of orcas date to the 18th century in Japan. During the 19th and early 20th centuries, the global [whaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whaling) industry caught immense numbers of [baleen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baleen_whale) and sperm whales, but largely ignored orcas because of their limited amounts of [recoverable oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_oil), their smaller populations, and the difficulty of taking them. [123] Once the stocks of larger species were depleted, orcas were targeted by commercial whalers in the mid-20th century. Between 1954 and 1997, Japan took 1,178 orcas (although the

[Ministry of the Environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Environment_(Japan))claims that there had been domestic catches of about 1,600 whales between the late 1940s to 1960s

) and Norway took 987.

[[226]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-227)Extensive hunting of orcas, including an

[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObeeEllis199234-228)[Antarctic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic)catch of 916 in 1979–80 alone, prompted the

[International Whaling Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Whaling_Commission)to recommend a ban on commercial hunting of the species pending further research.

Today, no country carries out a substantial hunt, although

[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObeeEllis199234-228)[Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia)and

[Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland)permit small subsistence hunts (see

[Aboriginal whaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aboriginal_whaling)). Other than commercial hunts, orcas were hunted along Japanese coasts out of public concern for potential conflicts with fisheries. Such cases include a semi-resident male-female pair in

[Akashi Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akashi_Strait)and

[Harimanada](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harimanada&action=edit&redlink=1)being killed in the

[Seto Inland Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seto_Inland_Sea)in 1957,


[[228]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-OSAKACEA-229)the killing of five whales from a pod of 11 members that swam into

[[229]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-230)[Tokyo Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokyo_Bay)in 1970,

and a catch record in southern Taiwan in the 1990s.

[[232]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-233)#### Cooperation with humans

Orcas have helped humans hunting other whales. [233] One well-known example was the

[orcas of Eden, Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_whales_of_Eden,_New_South_Wales), including the male known as

[Old Tom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Tom_(orca)). Whalers more often considered them a nuisance, however, as orcas would gather to scavenge meat from the whalers' catch.

Some populations, such as in Alaska's

[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-scavenging-234)[Prince William Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_William_Sound), may have been reduced significantly by whalers shooting them in retaliation.


[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-baird-21)### Whale watching

[Whale watching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_watching) continues to increase in popularity, but may have some problematic impacts on orcas. Exposure to exhaust gases from large amounts of vessel traffic is causing concern for the overall health of the 75 remaining southern resident orcas (SRKWs) left as of early 2019. [234] This population is followed by approximately 20 vessels for 12 hours a day during the months May–September.

Researchers discovered that these vessels are in the line of sight for these whales for 98–99.5% of daylight hours.

[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lachmuth_et_al._2011-236)With so many vessels, the air quality around these whales deteriorates and impacts their health. Air pollutants that bind with exhaust fumes are responsible for the activation of the cytochrome P450 1A gene family.

[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lachmuth_et_al._2011-236)Researchers have successfully identified this gene in skin biopsies of live whales and also the lungs of deceased whales. A direct correlation between activation of this gene and the air pollutants can not be made because there are other known factors that will induce the same gene. Vessels can have either wet or dry exhaust systems, with wet exhaust systems leaving more pollutants in the water due to various gas solubility. A modelling study determined that the lowest-observed-adverse-effect-level (LOAEL) of exhaust pollutants was about 12% of the human dose.

[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lachmuth_et_al._2011-236)

[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lachmuth_et_al._2011-236)As a response to this, in 2017 boats off the British Columbia coast now have a minimum approach distance of 200 metres compared to the previous 100 metres. This new rule complements Washington State's minimum approach zone of 180 metres that has been in effect since 2011. If a whale approaches a vessel it must be placed in neutral until the whale passes. The World Health Organization has set air quality standards in an effort to control the emissions produced by these vessels.[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-237)

### Captivity

The orca's [intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_intelligence), trainability, striking appearance, playfulness in captivity and sheer size have made it a popular exhibit at aquaria and [aquatic theme parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_mammal_park). From 1976 to 1997, 55 whales were taken from the wild in Iceland, 19 from Japan, and three from Argentina. These figures exclude animals that died during capture. Live captures fell dramatically in the 1990s, and by 1999, about 40% of the 48 animals on display in the world were captive-born.[[237]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTENMFS200543–44-238)

Organizations such as [World Animal Protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Animal_Protection) and [Whale and Dolphin Conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_and_Dolphin_Conservation) campaign against the practice of keeping them in captivity. In captivity, they often develop pathologies, such as the [dorsal fin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorsal_fin) collapse seen in 60–90% of captive males. Captives have vastly reduced life expectancies, on average only living into their 20s. [b] That said, a 2015 study coauthored by staff at

[SeaWorld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SeaWorld)and the

[Minnesota Zoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minnesota_Zoo)suggested no significant difference in survivorship between free-ranging and captive orcas.

However, in the wild, females who survive infancy live 46 years on average, and up to 70–80 years in rare cases. Wild males who survive infancy live 31 years on average, and up to 50–60 years.

[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-robeck-162)Captivity usually bears little resemblance to wild habitat, and captive whales' social groups are foreign to those found in the wild. Critics claim captive life is stressful due to these factors and the requirement to perform

[[238]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-240)[circus tricks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circus_trick)that are not part of wild orca behaviour, see

[above](https://en.wikipedia.org#Behaviour).

Wild orcas may travel up to 160 kilometres (100 mi) in a day, and critics say the animals are too big and intelligent to be suitable for captivity.

[[239]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-241)Captives occasionally act aggressively towards themselves, their tankmates, or humans, which critics say is a result of

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-cbs2010-143)[stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(medicine)).

Between 1991 and 2010, the bull orca known as

[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-abc2006-213)[Tilikum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilikum_(orca))was involved in the death of three people, and was featured in the critically acclaimed 2013 film

[.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackfish_(film))

*Blackfish*Tilikum lived at SeaWorld from 1992 until his death in 2017.
