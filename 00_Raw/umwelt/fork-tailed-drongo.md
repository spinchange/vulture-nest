*Dicrurus adsimilis*The **fork-tailed drongo** (* Dicrurus adsimilis*), also called the

**common drongo**or

**African drongo**, is a small bird found from the

[Sahel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahel)to

[South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa)that lives in wooded habitats, particularly

[woodlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodland)and

[savannas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna). It is part of the

[family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_(taxonomy))

[Dicruridae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicruridae)and has four recognized

[subspecies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspecies),

*D. a adsimilis*,

*D. a. apivorus*,

*D. a. fugax*and

*D. a. jubaensis*. Like other drongos, the fork-tailed is mostly

[insectivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insectivore); its diet mainly consists of

[butterflies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly),

[termites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Termite), and

[grasshoppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper).

Physically, this species is characterized with a narrow fork-shaped tail, red-brownish eyes, and black plumage throughout all of its body. These birds nest close to [wetlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetland), forests, and farms and the breeding season varies depending the region. The female usually lays one to four eggs, which hatch in 15 to 18 days.

The fork-tailed drongo is known for its ability to [deceptively mimic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deception_in_animals#Tactical_deception) other bird alarm calls in order for a certain animal to flee the scene so it can steal their food (kleptoparasitism). They are also known for their aggressive and fearless behavior, often attacking and driving away much larger animals, including birds of prey, when their nest is in danger. Due to its extensive range and stable population, the fork-tailed drongo is classified by the [IUCN Red List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List) as a [least-concern species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least-concern_species).

The fork-tailed drongo is a common and widespread resident breeder in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa) south of the [Sahara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahara). This [insect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insect)-eating bird is usually found in open [woodland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodland), [savanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna) and [forest edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodland_edge) and is tolerant of arid climates. [7] Its range was formerly considered to include Asia, but the Asian species is now called the

[black drongo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_drongo)(

*D. macrocercus*). The bird can be found at heights as high as 2200 meters.


[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:5-8)The bird possesses a robust black beak and red eyes. When born, they lack feathers, exhibiting reddish skin, an orange beak, yellow [gape flange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gape_flange), brown eyes, and a black beak. Juvenile fork-tailed drongos are dark brown tone with some buff-colored feather tips, a less pronounced tail fork, brown or grey eyes, and a pale mouth. While resembling adults, they lack the glossy shine on the lower body and display pale feathers on certain areas. Both sexes share a black body with a blue-green iridescence in specific regions. The underside, including the belly, is uniformly black, contrasting with the glossy black-blue upper body.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:5-8) [2] Their lengthy tail is deeply forked and black, measuring approximately 115–126 mm in length and 19–23 mm in depth. They feature short legs and a wingspan of 134 mm. Their bill is characterized by a depth of 0.4 mm and a length of 2.8 mm.


[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:5-8)

[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)Following breeding, adult birds experience a complete [molt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molt), usually taking place between December and March in Southern Africa, with varying months in other areas. Young birds maintain their immature plumage until the next breeding season. The partial post-juvenile molt initiates prior to the growth of new wing and tail feathers following the nesting period.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2) [9] Instances of

[leucism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leucism)have been observed in the fork-tailed drongo.


[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:6-10)This bird is notably vocal, often commencing the [dawn chorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawn_chorus_(birds)) and often is the last heard at dusk. [2] Their pre-dawn calls comprise variations such as

*jwaaa-jwaaa*and

*jeewy-jeeerr*. They exhibit a diverse repertoire of vocalizations, ranging from sharp calls, brief whistles, and squeaks to fluid, raspy, and scratchy notes delivered rapidly or with extended pauses. Their repertoire includes calls like

*chyup*,

*tjaaa*, the resounding

*jer-woo*, and the whistled

*jee-lu*. Mated pairs participate in coordinated duets lasting 4–5 minutes. Their songs feature soft, high-pitched, nasal, or melodious whistles, chirps, grinding sounds, and liquid chattering.


[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)These birds produce specific drongo calls and mimic other bird species[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2) [11] such as

[bocage's bushshrike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bocage%27s_bushshrike),

[thrushes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrushes),

[tchagras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tchagra),

[bulbuls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulbul),

[birds of prey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird_of_prey)and

[owls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owl). They have also been observed imitating the mewings of cats and the alarm calls of

[meerkats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meerkat).


[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)This species is mostly [insectivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insectivore) and occasionally eats fishes and other birds, furthermore they may take nectar and eat plants when available too.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-carn-12) [13] Predominant preyed animals are

[butterflies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly),

[beetles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beetle),

[caterpillars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caterpillar),

[grasshoppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper),

[honey bees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honey_bee),

[moths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moth),

[termites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Termite)and

[weevils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weevil), especially common species being the

[angola white lady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angola_white_lady),

*,*

[Macrotermes natalensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrotermes_natalensis)*, and the*

[Cyrtacanthacris aeruginosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrtacanthacris_aeruginosa)[desert locust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert_locust). Sometimes they might also eat small fishes by swooping down from a perch hovering over water and dipping, it has been observed small birds being captured with their claws or bills, namely the

[bronze mannikin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_mannikin). Plants take around 15% of their diet, usually eaten are the

*,*

[Moringa oleifera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moringa_oleifera)*and the*

[Azadirachta indica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azadirachta_indica)*. This bird is solitary in its hunting.*

[Dialium guineense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialium_guineense)

[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)This species spends about 62% of the day feeding during the dry season, and 56% of the day during rainy seasons.

[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Okosodo2016-13)

[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Okosodo2016-13)Typically, the fork-tailed drongo perches at a height ranging from 5 m (16 ft 5 in) to 7 m (22 ft 11+1⁄2 in), adopting an erect posture from which they swiftly pursue insects by flycatching, plunge diving, or seizing them on the ground before returning to the same branch. This species is capable of holding large items with their claws and rending them with their bills. They frequently inhabit savanna fires, where they capture fleeing insects and other prey seeking refuge from the flames. [13] These birds maintain a

[commensalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensalism)relationship with large mammals, trailing animals such as elephants and giraffes that disturb insects in the vicinity, thereby flushing out potential prey.


[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-14)A tactic employed by the fork-tailed drongo to acquire food is an opportunistic [kleptoparasitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleptoparasitism). The drongo will give genuine alarm calls to signal the presence of predators to other animals, but occasionally, it will issue a false alarm call to displace those animals and steal their food. [11] These birds may also engage in direct attacks on other species or do so after a failed false alarm attempt.


[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-15)It has been observed that fork-tailed drongos spend approximately 29% of their time trailing other animals. [16] Species such as

[southern pied babblers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_pied_babbler),

[sociable weavers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociable_weaver),

[wattled starlings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wattled_starling)and

[meerkats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meerkat), which forage on the ground, are often targeted by fork-tailed's kleptoparasitism. This behavior arises because drongos lack the necessary morphological adaptations to effectively hunt certain prey that are more nutritious and calorific, hence resorting to kleptoparasitism.


[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-17)These birds frequently assume leadership roles in

[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:4-18)[mixed-species foraging flocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-species_foraging_flock), serving as sentinels alongside other species. This cooperative strategy reduces the risk of predation and enhances the foraging success of the associated species. Simultaneously, the drongo exploits these associations to increase opportunities for kleptoparasitism.


[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:2-7)

[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:4-18)Although in doubt, researchers have considered the possibility that these drongos possess a [theory of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_mind_in_animals), a trait not fully demonstrated in any animal other than humans. This is attributed to the birds' adaptability in response to feedback. They may vary their tactics by occasionally emitting either genuine or deceptive alarm calls.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nationalgeographic-19) [20] Approximately a quarter of their food intake is estimated to result from kleptoparasitism, with an additional 10% acquired by capturing prey flushed by associated species. Furthermore, the prey caught using this strategy are typically larger than those acquired through self-foraging.

After emitting a deceptive call, the drongo will follow up with an 'all clear' signal, to minimize the disruption to the foraging activities of other animals and increases the frequency of genuine alarm calls in order to gain the trust back.

[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:3-16)

[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:4-18)A study has cast doubt on the widespread assumption that the fork-tailed drongo relies heavily on its kleptoparasitic tactics. Instead, these birds may deploy such strategies primarily in times of food scarcity, such as during droughts and on cold days. [10] Even though the

[biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass)intake when doing kleptoparasitism is higher, it also poses risks as a foraging tactic and may result in additional foraging costs compared to self-foraging.


[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:3-16)During the breeding season, these birds often nest close to [wetlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetlands), forests, and farms, benefiting from the favorable nesting sites and materials provided by the [microhabitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microhabitat) and vegetation. [13] They exhibit

[monogamous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogamy_in_animals)behavior and are known for their aggressiveness towards other drongos, as well as nest predators like crows, birds of prey,

[hornbills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hornbills),

[shrikes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shrikes), small mammals, and large snakes. Partners perch together, performing duets, and displaying behaviors such as tilting and nodding their heads.

[Brood parasitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brood_parasitism)on

[groundscraper thrush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundscraper_thrush)have been observed.


[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)The breeding season typically extends from March to September north of the equator and from September to January south of it. The timing of the egg-laying season varies significantly between regions. The number of broods ranges from one to four, and if a [clutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clutch) is lost early in the season, they may replace it. Nests are usually positioned 2.2 to 17 meters above the ground, constructed between horizontal branches, and feature [saucer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saucer)-shaped structures crafted from plant stems, lichens, small roots, [tendrils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tendril), and [cobwebs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobwebs). Eggs are laid at intervals of 24 to 48 hours. Incubation, lasting 15 to 18 days, begins only after the clutch is complete. Both male and female birds feed the chicks, providing flies, beetle larvae, lizards, and seeds. The [nestling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nestling) period typically spans from 16 to 22 days.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hbw-2)

Drongos are frequently utilized as brood hosts by [African cuckoos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_cuckoo) (21.8% of nests), and in the [Kalahari Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalahari_Desert), it has been discovered that [Jacobin cuckoos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobin_cuckoo) also parasitize drongo nests. [21] Drongo eggs display a diverse array of colors and patterns, which the cuckoos mimic. Experiments indicate that drongos can detect and reject 93.7% of introduced eggs.


[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-22)Due to their very large range, stable population trend and size, the fork-tailed drongo is considered to be a [least-concern species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least-concern_species) by the [IUCN Red List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List), though the subspecies *D. a. modestus,* previously considered a separated species, was considered a [near-threatened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-threatened_species). The fork-tailed drongo faces threats from pesticide use, which diminishes their prey availability, and [habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction) due to farmland expansion. [1] However, the species is unlikely to be threatened in the near future as it benefits from tree clearance in dense forests.
