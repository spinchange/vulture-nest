The **chimpanzee** ([/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English) /; * Pan troglodytes*), also simply known as the

**chimp**, is an

[endangered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered)species of

[great ape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hominidae)native to the forests and savannahs of

[tropical Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_Africa). It has four confirmed subspecies and a fifth proposed one. When its close relative, the

[bonobo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonobo), was more commonly known as the pygmy chimpanzee, this species was often called the

**common chimpanzee**or the

**robust chimpanzee**. The chimpanzee and the bonobo are the only species in the genus

[.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_(genus))

*Pan*The chimpanzee is covered in coarse black hair but has a bare face, fingers, toes, palms of the hands, and soles of the feet. It is larger and more robust than the bonobo, weighing 40–70 kg (88–154 lb) for males and 27–50 kg (60–110 lb) for females and standing 150 cm (4 ft 11 in). Evidence from fossils and [DNA sequencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_sequencing) shows that *Pan* is a [sister taxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sister_taxon) to the [human lineage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_evolution) and is thus [humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human)' closest living relative. The chimpanzee [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome) contains coding regions for 18,759 proteins which is within ten percent compared to 20,383 proteins for humans.

The chimpanzee lives in groups that range in size from 15 to 150 members, although individuals travel and forage in much smaller groups during the day. The species lives in a strict male-dominated hierarchy, where disputes are generally settled without the need for violence. Nearly all chimpanzee populations have been recorded [using tools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals), modifying sticks, rocks, grass and leaves and using them for [hunting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting) and acquiring [honey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honey), [termites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Termite), [ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant), nuts and water. The species has also been found creating sharpened sticks to spear small mammals. Its [gestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pregnancy) period is eight months. The infant is [weaned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weaning) at about three years old but usually maintains a close relationship with its mother for several years more.

The chimpanzee is listed on the [IUCN Red List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List) as an endangered species. Between 170,000 and 300,000 individuals are estimated across its range. The biggest threats to the chimpanzee are habitat loss, [poaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poaching), and disease. Chimpanzees appear in Western popular culture as stereotyped clown-figures and have featured in entertainments such as [chimpanzees' tea parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chimpanzees%27_tea_party), circus acts and stage shows. Although chimpanzees have been kept as pets, their strength, aggressiveness, and unpredictability makes them dangerous in this role. Some hundreds have been kept in laboratories for research, especially in the United States. Many experimental attempts have been made to attempt teaching languages such as [American Sign Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Sign_Language) to chimpanzees, which have been challenged by academics such as [Noam Chomsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noam_Chomsky).

The first great ape known to Western science in the 17th century was the "[orang-outang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bornean_orangutan)" (genus *Pongo*), the local [Malay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_language) name being recorded in Java by the Dutch physician [Jacobus Bontius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobus_Bontius). In 1641, the Dutch anatomist [Nicolaes Tulp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaes_Tulp) applied the name to a chimpanzee or bonobo brought to the Netherlands from Angola. [13] Another Dutch anatomist,

[Peter Camper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Camper), dissected specimens from Central Africa and Southeast Asia in the 1770s, noting the differences between the African and Asian apes. The German naturalist

[Johann Friedrich Blumenbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Blumenbach)classified the chimpanzee as

*Simia troglodytes*by 1775. Another German naturalist,

[Lorenz Oken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorenz_Oken), coined the genus

*Pan*in 1816. The bonobo was recognised as distinct from the chimpanzee by 1933.

The DNA sequences of humans and chimpanzees are very similar and the difference in protein number mostly arises from incomplete sequences in the chimpanzee genome. Both species differ by about 35 million

Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes and other great apes, such as chimpanzees, have 24 pairs of chromosomes. In the human evolutionary lineage, two ancestral ape chromosomes fused at their

[telomeres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telomere), producing human

[chromosome 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome_2).

Another study showed that patterns of

[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-44)[DNA methylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_methylation), which are a known regulation mechanism for gene expression, differ in the prefrontal cortex of humans versus chimpanzees, and implicated this difference in the evolutionary divergence of the two species.


[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-45)The published chimpanzee genome differs from that of the human genome by 1.23% in direct sequence comparisons. [45] Around 20% of this figure is accounted for by variation within each species, leaving only ~1.06% consistent sequence divergence between humans and chimps at shared genes.

This nucleotide by nucleotide difference is dwarfed, however, by the portion of each genome that is not shared, including around 6% of functional genes that are unique to either humans or chimps.

[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-47)In other words, the considerable observable differences between humans and chimps may be due as much or more to genome level variation in the number, function and expression of genes rather than DNA sequence changes in shared genes. On average, a typical human protein-coding gene differs from its chimpanzee

[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-48)[ortholog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ortholog)by only two

[amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid)substitutions; nearly one third of human genes have exactly the same protein translation as their chimpanzee orthologs. A major difference between the two genomes is human

[chromosome 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome_2), which is equivalent to a fusion product of chimpanzee chromosomes 12 and 13 (later renamed to chromosomes 2A and 2B, respectively).

In comparing the human genome to that of both chimpanzees and bonobos, a 2021 study by Yafei Mao found that the human genome was closer to the chimpanzee in same segments (2.55%), and the bonobo in others (2.52%).

[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-50)## Characteristics

Adult chimpanzees have an average standing height of 150 cm (4 ft 11 in). [50] Wild adult males weigh between 40 and 70 kg (88 and 154 lb),


[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-52)

[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-53)and females weigh between 27 and 50 kg (60 and 110 lb).

[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-54)In exceptional cases, certain individuals may considerably exceed these measurements, standing over 168 cm (5 ft 6 in) on two legs and weighing up to 136 kg (300 lb) in captivity.

[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:0-55)The chimpanzee is more robustly built than the

[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-58)[bonobo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonobo)but less than the

[gorilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gorilla). The arms of a chimpanzee are longer than its legs and can reach below the knees. The hands have long fingers with short thumbs and flat fingernails. The feet are adapted for grasping, and the big toe is

[opposable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thumb#Opposition_and_apposition). The pelvis is long with an extended

[ilium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilium_(bone)). A chimpanzee's head is rounded with a prominent and

[prognathous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prognathous)face and a pronounced

[brow ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brow_ridge). It has forward-facing eyes, a small nose, rounded non-lobed ears and a long mobile upper lip. Additionally, adult males have sharp canine teeth. Like all great apes, it has a

[dental formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dental_formula)of 2.1.2.3, that is, two

[incisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incisors), one

[canine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canine_tooth), two

[premolars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premolars), and three

[molars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_(tooth))on both halves of each jaw. Chimpanzees lack the prominent

[sagittal crest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittal_crest)and associated head and neck musculature of gorillas.


[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jones_etal.1996-14)

[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Estes-59)Chimpanzee bodies are covered by coarse hair, except for the face, fingers, toes, palms of the hands, and soles of the feet. Chimpanzees lose more hair as they age and develop bald spots. The hair of a chimpanzee is typically black but can be brown or ginger. As they get older, white or grey patches may appear, particularly on the chin and lower region.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jones_etal.1996-14) [57] Chimpanzee skin that is covered with body hair is white, while exposed areas vary: white which ages into a dark muddy colour in eastern chimpanzees, freckled on white which ages to a heavily mottled muddy colour in central chimpanzees, and black with a butterfly-shaped white mask that darkens with age in western chimpanzees.


[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Post_Szabó_Keeling_1975-60)Facial pigmentation increases with age and exposure to ultraviolet light. Females develop swelling pink skin when in oestrus.

[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Napier_Napier_1967-61)

[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Estes-59)[penis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penis)with a small

[baculum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baculum), but without a

[glans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glans_penis).


[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-62)Chimpanzees are adapted for both [arboreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arboreal_locomotion) and [terrestrial locomotion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_locomotion). Arboreal locomotion consists of vertical climbing and [brachiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brachiation).[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-63) [62] On the ground, chimpanzees move both quadrupedally and bipedally. These movements appear to have similar energy costs.

As with bonobos and gorillas, chimpanzees move quadrupedally by

[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-65)[knuckle-walking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knuckle-walking), which probably evolved independently in

*Pan*and

[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hopkins2010-74)## Ecology

The chimpanzee is a highly adaptable species. It lives in a variety of habitats, including dry [savanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna), [evergreen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evergreen) rainforest, [montane forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montane_forest), [swamp forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swamp_forest), and dry [woodland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodland)-savanna mosaic.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Poulsen2004-75) [73] In

[Gombe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gombe_Stream_National_Park), the chimpanzee mostly uses

[semideciduous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semideciduous)and evergreen forest as well as open woodland.

At

[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall198649-77)[Bossou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bossou), the chimpanzee inhabits multistage secondary

[deciduous forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deciduous_forest), which has grown after

[shifting cultivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shifting_cultivation), as well as

[primary forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old-growth_forest)and grassland.

At

[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-78)[Taï](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ta%C3%AF_National_Park), it is found in the last remaining tropical rain forest in Ivory Coast.

The chimpanzee has an advanced

[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-79)[cognitive map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_map)of its home range and can repeatedly find food.

The chimpanzee builds a sleeping nest in a tree in a different location each night, never using the same nest more than once. Chimpanzees sleep alone in separate nests except for infants or juvenile chimpanzees, which sleep with their mothers.

[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-97)### Mortality and health

The average lifespan of a wild chimpanzee is relatively short. They usually live less than 15 years, although individuals that reach 12 years may live an additional 15 years. On rare occasions, wild chimpanzees may live nearly 60 years. Captive chimpanzees tend to live longer than most wild ones, with median lifespans of 31.7 years for males and 38.7 years for females. [95] The oldest-known male captive chimpanzee to have been documented lived to 66 years,

Chimpanzees may react to a leopard's presence with loud vocalising, branch shaking, and throwing objects.

[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-107)## Behaviour

Behaviour of chimpanzees has been studied from multiple perspectives including their group structure, their mating preferences, their parenting capacities, the limits of their communication abilities, their hunting abilities and their ritual behaviour.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-harrodStudy-108)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-109)

### Group structure

Chimpanzees live in communities that typically range from around 15 to more than 150 members but spend most of their time traveling in small, temporary groups consisting of a few individuals. These groups may consist of any combination of age and sexes. Both males and females sometimes travel alone. [78] This

[fission–fusion society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission%E2%80%93fusion_society)may include groups of four types: all-male, adult females and offspring, adults of both sexes, or one female and her offspring. These smaller groups emerge in a variety of types, for a variety of purposes. For example, an all-male troop may be organised to

[hunt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting)for meat, while a group consisting of lactating females serves to act as a "nursery group" for the young.

At the core of social structures are males, which patrol the territory, protect group members, and search for food. Males remain in their natal communities, while females generally emigrate at adolescence. Males in a community are more likely to be related to one another than females are to each other. Among males, there is generally a dominance hierarchy, and males are dominant over females.

[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pepper-110)However, this unusual fission-fusion social structure, "in which portions of the parent group may on a regular basis separate from and then rejoin the rest",

[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-111)is highly variable in terms of which particular individual chimpanzees congregate at a given time. This is caused mainly by the large measure of individual autonomy that individuals have within their fission-fusion social groups.

[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986147-112)As a result, individual chimpanzees often forage for food alone, or in smaller groups, as opposed to the much larger "parent" group, which encompasses all the chimpanzees which regularly come into contact with each other and congregate into parties in a particular area.

[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Estes-59)

[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pepper-110)Male chimpanzees exist in a linear [dominance hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy). Top-ranking males tend to be aggressive even during dominance stability. [110] This is probably due to the chimpanzee's fission-fusion society, with male chimpanzees leaving groups and returning after extended periods of time. With this, a dominant male is unsure if any "political maneuvering" has occurred in his absence and must re-establish his dominance. Thus, a large amount of aggression occurs within five to fifteen minutes after a reunion. During these encounters, displays of aggression are generally preferred over physical attacks.


[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Muller2002-113)Males maintain and improve their social ranks by forming coalitions, which have been characterised as "exploitative" and based on an individual's influence in agonistic interactions.

[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Bygott1979-114)Being in a coalition allows males to dominate a third individual when they could not by themselves, as politically apt chimpanzees can exert power over aggressive interactions regardless of their rank. Coalitions can also give an individual male the confidence to challenge a dominant or larger male. The more allies a male has, the better his chance of becoming dominant. However, most changes in hierarchical rank are caused by

[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-deWaal1987-115)[dyadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyad_(sociology))interactions.


[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Muller2002-113)Chimpanzee alliances can be very fickle, and one member may suddenly turn on another if it is to his advantage.

[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Watts2001-116)

[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Nishida1986-117)Low-ranking males frequently switch sides in disputes between more dominant individuals. Low-ranking males benefit from an unstable hierarchy and often find increased sexual opportunities if a dispute or conflict occurs.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-deWaal1987-115) [114] In addition, conflicts between dominant males cause them to focus on each other rather than the lower-ranking males. Social hierarchies among adult females tend to be weaker. Nevertheless, the status of an adult female may be important for her offspring.

Females in

[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pusey1997-118)[Taï](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ta%C3%AF_National_Park)have also been recorded to form alliances.

While chimpanzee social structure is often referred to as

[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-119)[patriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchal), it is not entirely unheard of for females to forge coalitions against males.

There is also at least one recorded case of females securing a dominant position over males in their respective troop, albeit in a captive environment.

[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-120)

[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-121)[Social grooming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_grooming)appears to be important in the formation and maintenance of coalitions. It is more common among adult males than either between adult females or between males and females.


[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Watts2001-116)Chimpanzees have been described as highly territorial and will frequently kill other chimpanzees, [119] although Margaret Power wrote in her 1991 book

*The Egalitarians*that the field studies from which the aggressive data came, Gombe and Mahale, used artificial feeding systems that increased aggression in the chimpanzee populations studied. Thus, the behaviour may not reflect innate characteristics of the species as a whole.

In the years following her artificial feeding conditions at Gombe, Jane Goodall described groups of male chimpanzees patrolling the borders of their territory, brutally attacking chimpanzees that had split off from the Gombe group. A study published in 2010 found that the chimpanzees wage wars over territory, not mates.

[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Power.Anthropologist-123)Patrols from smaller groups are more likely to avoid contact with their neighbours. Patrols from large groups even take over a smaller group's territory, gaining access to more resources, food, and females.

[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-124)

[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Nishida1986-117)While it was traditionally accepted that only female chimpanzees immigrate and males remain in their natal troop for life, there are confirmed cases of adult males safely integrating themselves into new communities among West African chimpanzees, suggesting they are less territorial than other subspecies.

[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986491,_528-125)West African chimpanzee males are also less aggressive with female chimpanzees in general.

[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-126)While chimpanzees are popularly thought of as more war-like than bonobos, primatologist Christine Webb concludes that "Through an analysis of various chimpanzee and bonobo sanctuary communities, we've found that variation

[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-127)*within*the two species is greater than differences

*between*them".


[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-128)### Mating and parenting

Chimpanzees mate throughout the year, although the number of females in [oestrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oestrus) varies seasonally in a group. [126] Female chimpanzees are more likely to come into oestrus when food is readily available. Oestrous females exhibit

[sexual swellings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_swelling). Chimpanzees are

[promiscuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promiscuous); during oestrus, females mate with several males in their community, while males have large testicles for

[sperm competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_competition). Other forms of mating also exist. A community's dominant males sometimes restrict reproductive access to females. A male and female can form a consortship and mate outside their community. In addition, females sometimes leave their community and mate with males from neighboring communities.


[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986450–451-130)These

[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gagneux1999-131)[alternative mating strategies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_mating_strategy)give females more mating opportunities without losing the support of the males in their community.


[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gagneux1999-131)[Infanticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infanticide_in_primates)has been recorded in chimpanzee communities in some areas, and the victims are often consumed. Male chimpanzees practice infanticide on unrelated young to shorten the interbirth intervals in the females.


[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-132)Females sometimes practice infanticide. This may be related to the dominance hierarchy in females or may simply be pathological.

[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Goodall1977-133)

[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pusey1997-118)[Inbreeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inbreeding) was studied in a relatively undisturbed eastern chimpanzee community that displayed substantial bisexual [philopatry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philopatry). [131] Despite an increased inbreeding risk incurred by females who do not disperse before reaching reproductive age, these females were still able to

[avoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inbreeding_avoidance)producing inbred offspring.


[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-White2024-134)[Copulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copulation_(zoology))is brief, lasting approximately seven seconds.

The

[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Dixson2012-135)[gestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestation)period is eight months.

Care for the young is provided mostly by their mothers. The survival and emotional health of the young is dependent on maternal care. Mothers provide their young with food, warmth, and protection, and teach them certain skills. In addition, a chimpanzee's future rank may be dependent on its mother's status.

[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Estes-59)

[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986203–205-136)Male chimpanzees continue to associate with the females they impregnated and interact with and support their offspring.

[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Foerster_Franz_Murray_Gilby-137)Orphaned chimpanzees are occasionally adopted by adult males who will be "as protective as any mother" and tend to their needs.

[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-138)

[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-139)Newborn chimpanzees are helpless. For example, their grasping reflex is not strong enough to support them for more than a few seconds. For their first 30 days, infants cling to their mother's bellies. Infants are unable to support their own weight for their first two months and need their mothers' support. [137] Wild chimps are seen to exhibit both "secure" and "insecure" attachment styles, with the offspring looking to the mother for comfort in the former and more independent offspring in the latter. However, wild chimps rarely demonstrate "disorganised" attachment styles (maladaptive parent-offspring bonds caused by abuse or neglect); researchers note such attachment styles are mostly observed in captive chimps raised around humans.


[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-141)When they reach five to six months, infants ride on their mothers' backs. They remain in continual contact for the rest of their first year. When they reach two years of age, they are able to move and sit independently and start moving beyond the arms' reach of their mothers. By four to six years, chimpanzees are weaned and infancy ends. The juvenile period for chimpanzees lasts from their sixth to ninth years. Juveniles remain close to their mothers but increasingly begin to interact with other members of their community. Adolescent females move between groups and are supported by their mothers in agonistic encounters. Adolescent males spend time with adult males in social activities like hunting and patrolling the boundaries of their territory. [137] A captive study suggests males can safely immigrate to a new group if accompanied by immigrant females who have an existing relationship with this male. This gives the resident males reproductive advantages with these females, as they are more inclined to remain in the group if their male friend is also accepted.


[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-142)### Communication

Chimpanzees use facial expressions, postures, and sounds to communicate with each other. Chimpanzees have expressive faces that are important in close-up communications. When frightened, a "full closed grin" causes nearby individuals to be fearful, as well. Playful chimpanzees display an open-mouthed grin. Chimpanzees may also express themselves with the "pout", which is made in distress, the "sneer", which is made when threatening or fearful, and "compressed-lips face", which is a type of display. When submitting to a dominant individual, a chimpanzee crunches, bobs, and extends a hand. When in an aggressive mode, a chimpanzee swaggers bipedally, hunched over and arms waving, in an attempt to exaggerate its size. [141] While travelling, chimpanzees keep in contact by beating their hands and feet against the trunks of large trees, an act that is known as "drumming". They also do this when encountering individuals from other communities.


[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Crockford2005-145)Vocalisations are also important in chimpanzee communication. The most common call in adults is the "[pant-hoot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pant-hoot_(call))", which may signal social rank and bond along with keeping groups together. Pant-hoots are made of four parts, starting with soft "hoos", the introduction; that gets louder and louder, the build-up; and climax into screams and sometimes barks; these die down back to soft "hoos" during the letdown phase as the call ends.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Fedurek-143) [142] Grunting is made in situations like feeding and greeting.

Submissive individuals make "pant-grunts" towards their superiors.

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Crockford2005-145)

[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pusey1997-118)Whimpering is made by young chimpanzees as a form of begging or when lost from the group.

[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986129-146)Chimpanzees use distance calls to draw attention to danger, food sources, or other community members.

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Crockford2005-145)"Barks" may be made as "short barks" when hunting and "tonal barks" when sighting large snakes.

[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986132–133-147)

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Crockford2005-145)### Hunting

When hunting small monkeys such as the [red colobus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_red_colobus), chimpanzees hunt where the forest canopy is interrupted or irregular. This allows them to easily corner the monkeys when chasing them in the appropriate direction. Chimpanzees may also hunt as a coordinated team, so that they can corner their prey even in a continuous canopy. During an [arboreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arboreal) hunt, each chimpanzee in the hunting groups has a role. "Drivers" serve to keep the prey running in a certain direction and follow them without attempting to make a catch. "Blockers" are stationed at the bottom of the trees and climb up to block prey that takes off in a different direction. "Chasers" move quickly and try to make a catch. Finally, "ambushers" hide and rush out when a monkey nears. [145] While both adults and infants are taken, adult male colobus monkeys will attack the hunting chimps.

When caught and killed, the meal is distributed to all hunting party members and even bystanders.

[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodall1986273–274-149)

[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Leipzig2002-148)Male chimpanzees hunt in groups more than females. Female chimpanzees tend to hunt solitarily. If a female chimpanzee were to participate in the hunting group and catch a Red Colobus, it would likely immediately be taken by an adult male. Female chimpanzees are estimated to hunt ≈ 10-15% of a community's vertebrates.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-150)

tool use,

[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-recruit-152)and varied

[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch1993-153)[language capabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_ape_language).

They are among species that have passed the

[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-GAT-154)[mirror test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_test), suggesting

[self-awareness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-awareness).

In one study, two young chimpanzees showed retention of mirror self-recognition after one year without access to mirrors.

[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-155)Chimpanzees have been observed to use insects to treat their own wounds and those of others. They catch them and apply them directly to the injury.

[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-156)Chimpanzees also display signs of

[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-157)[culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_culture)among groups, with the learning and transmission of variations in grooming, tool use and foraging techniques leading to local traditions.

A 30-year study at

[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-158)[Kyoto University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyoto_University)'s

[Primate Research Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primate_Research_Institute)has shown that chimpanzees are able to learn to recognise the numbers 1 to 9 and their values. The chimpanzees further show an aptitude for

[eidetic memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eidetic_memory), demonstrated in experiments in which the jumbled digits are flashed onto a computer screen for less than a quarter of a second. One chimpanzee,

[Ayumu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayumu_(chimpanzee)), was able to correctly and quickly point to the positions where they appeared in ascending order. Ayumu performed better than human adults who were given the same test.


[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-symbol-151)In controlled [experiments on cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative_pulling_paradigm), chimpanzees show a basic understanding of cooperation, and recruit the best collaborators. [149] In a group setting with a device that delivered food rewards only to cooperating chimpanzees, cooperation first increased, then, due to competitive behaviour, decreased, before finally increasing to the highest level through punishment and other arbitrage behaviour.

Great apes show

[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Suchak-159)[laughter-like vocalisations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laughter_in_animals)in response to physical contact, such as wrestling, play chasing, or

[tickling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tickling). This is documented in wild and captive chimpanzees. Chimpanzee laughter is not readily recognisable to humans as such, because it is generated by alternating inhalations and exhalations that sound more like breathing and panting. Instances in which nonhuman primates have expressed joy have been reported. Humans and chimpanzees share similar ticklish areas of the body, such as the armpits and belly. The enjoyment of tickling in chimpanzees does not diminish with age.


[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Discover2003-160)A 2022 study reported that chimpanzees crushed and applied insects to their own wounds and the wounds of other chimpanzees. [158] Chimpanzees display different behaviour in response to a dying or dead group member. When witnessing a sudden death, the other group members act in frenzy, with vocalisations, aggressive displays, and touching of the corpse. In one case chimpanzees cared for a dying elder, and then attended and cleaned the corpse. Afterward, they avoided the spot where the elder had died, and behaved in a more subdued manner.

Mothers have been reported to carry around and groom their dead infants for several days.

[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-162)Experimenters now and then witness behaviour that cannot be readily reconciled with chimpanzee intelligence or

[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-163)[theory of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_mind).

[Wolfgang Köhler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_K%C3%B6hler), for instance, reported insightful behaviour in chimpanzees, but he likewise often observed that they experienced "special difficulty" in solving simple problems.

Researchers also reported that, when faced with a choice between two persons, chimpanzees were just as likely to beg food from a person who could see the begging gesture as from a person who could not, thereby raising the possibility that chimpanzees lack

[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-164)[theory of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_mind).

By contrast, Hare, Call, and Tomasello found that subordinate chimpanzees were able to use the knowledge state of dominant rival chimpanzees to determine which container of hidden food they approached.

[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hare-2001-166)### Tool use

Nearly all chimpanzee populations have been recorded using tools. They modify sticks, rocks, grass, and leaves and use them when foraging for termites and ants, [164] nuts,


[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:1-167)

[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-168)

[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-169)honey,

[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-170)algae

[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-171)or water. Despite the lack of complexity, forethought and skill are apparent in making these tools.

[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-172)Chimpanzees have used stone tools since at least 4,300 years ago.

[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch1993-153)

[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-173)A chimpanzee from the [Kasakela chimpanzee community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasakela_chimpanzee_community) was the first nonhuman animal reported making a tool, by modifying a twig to use as an instrument for extracting termites from their mound.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Goodall_1971-174) [172] At Taï, chimpanzees simply use their hands to extract termites.

When foraging for honey, chimpanzees use modified short sticks to scoop the honey out of the hive if the bees are

[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch1993-153)[stingless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stingless_bees). For hives of the dangerous

[African honeybees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_honeybee), chimpanzees use longer and thinner sticks to extract the honey.

Chimpanzees also fish for ants using the same tactic.

[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-176)Ant dipping is difficult and some chimpanzees never master it.

[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch_Boesch_1982-177)[West African chimpanzees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_chimpanzee)crack open hard nuts with stones or branches.


[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch1993-153)Some forethought in this activity is apparent, as these tools are not found together or where the nuts are collected. Nut cracking is also difficult and must be learned.

[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch_Boesch_1982-177)Chimpanzees also use leaves as sponges or spoons to drink water.

[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Boesch_Boesch_1982-177)

[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-178)West African chimpanzees in Senegal were found to sharpen sticks with their teeth, which were then used to spear [Senegal bushbabies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senegal_bushbaby) out of small holes in trees. [176] An eastern chimpanzee has been observed using a modified branch as a tool to capture a

[squirrel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squirrel).

Chimpanzees living in Tanzania were found to deliberately choose plants that provide materials that produce more flexible tools for termite fishing.

[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-180)Whilst experimental studies on captive chimpanzees have found that many of their species-typical tool-use behaviour can be individually learnt by each chimpanzees,

[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-181)a 2021 study on their abilities to make and use stone flakes, in a similar way as hypothesised for early hominins, did not find this behaviour across two populations of chimpanzees—suggesting that this behaviour is outside the chimpanzee species-typical range.

[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-183)### Speech and language

Scientists have attempted to teach human [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) to several species of great ape. One early attempt by Allen and Beatrix Gardner in the 1960s involved spending 51 months teaching [American Sign Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Sign_Language) to a chimpanzee named [Washoe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washoe_(chimpanzee)). The Gardners reported that Washoe learned 151 signs, and had spontaneously taught them to other chimpanzees, including her adopted son, [Loulis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loulis). [181] Over a longer period of time, Washoe was reported to have learned over 350 signs.


[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-185)Debate is ongoing among scientists such as [David Premack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Premack) about chimpanzees' ability to learn language. Since the early reports on Washoe, numerous other studies have been conducted, with varying levels of success. [151] One involved a chimpanzee jokingly named

[Nim Chimpsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nim_Chimpsky)(in allusion to the theorist of language

[Noam Chomsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noam_Chomsky)), trained by Herbert Terrace of

[Columbia University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_University). Although his initial reports were quite positive, in November 1979, Terrace and his team, including psycholinguist

[Thomas Bever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Bever), re-evaluated the videotapes of Nim with his trainers, analyzing them frame by frame for signs, as well as for exact context (what was happening both before and after Nim's signs). In the reanalysis, Terrace and Bever concluded that Nim's utterances could be explained merely as prompting on the part of the experimenters, as well as mistakes in reporting the data. "Much of the apes' behaviour is pure drill", he said. "Language still stands as an important definition of the human species". In this reversal, Terrace now argued Nim's use of ASL was not like human

[language acquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_acquisition). Nim never initiated conversations himself, rarely introduced new words, and mostly imitated what the humans did. More importantly, Nim's word strings varied in their ordering, suggesting that he was incapable of

[syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax). Nim's sentences also did not grow in length, unlike human children whose vocabulary and sentence length show a strong positive correlation.

Chomsky has voiced opposition to the viewpoint of Terrace and Bever based upon his theory of the inheritance of language capacity as being limited to human inheritance alone among all animals.

[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-192)### As pets

Chimpanzees have traditionally been kept as pets in a few African villages, especially in the [Democratic Republic of Congo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo). In [Virunga National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virunga_National_Park) in the east of the country, the park authorities regularly seize chimpanzees from people keeping them as pets. [190] Outside their range, chimpanzees are popular as

[exotic pets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_pet)despite their strength and aggression. Even in places where keeping non-human primates as pets is illegal, the exotic pet trade continues to prosper, leading to injuries from attacks.


[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-194)### Use in research

Hundreds of chimpanzees have been kept in laboratories for research. Most such laboratories either conduct or make the animals available for invasive research, [192] defined as "inoculation with an infectious agent, surgery or biopsy conducted for the sake of research and not for the sake of the chimpanzee, and/or drug testing".

Research chimpanzees tend to be used repeatedly over decades for up to 40 years, unlike the pattern of use of most laboratory animals.

[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HSUSresearch-196)Two federally funded American laboratories use chimpanzees: the

[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HSUSbetter-197)[Yerkes National Primate Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yerkes_National_Primate_Research_Center)at

[Emory University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emory_University)in Atlanta, Georgia, and the Southwest National Primate Center in San Antonio, Texas.

Five hundred chimpanzees have been retired from laboratory use in the US and live in

[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lovgren-198)[animal sanctuaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_sanctuary)in the US or Canada.

A five-year moratorium was imposed by the US National Institutes of Health in 1996, because too many chimpanzees had been bred for HIV research, and it has been extended annually since 2001.

[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HSUSmap-195)With the publication of the

[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lovgren-198)[chimpanzee genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chimpanzee_genome), plans to increase the use of chimpanzees in America were reportedly increasing in 2006, some scientists arguing that the federal moratorium on breeding chimpanzees for research should be lifted.


[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Lovgren-198)However, in 2007, the NIH made the moratorium permanent.

[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Langley15-199)

[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Dunham-200)Other researchers argue that chimpanzees either should not be used in research, or should be treated differently, for instance with [legal status as persons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_ape_personhood). A recent study suggests chimpanzees which are retired from labs exhibit a form of

[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Wilson_et_al._(2020)-212)Goodall research common chimpanzee social and family life beginning with the [Kasakela chimpanzee community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kasakela_chimpanzee_community) in [Gombe Stream National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gombe_Stream_National_Park), [Tanzania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanzania), in 1960, contained much original commentary about their experienced emotional life.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-timeline-213) [211] She found that "it isn't only human beings who have personality, who are capable of rational thought [and] emotions like joy and sorrow".

[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-PBS-214)### Attacks

Chimpanzees have attacked humans.[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-215) [213] In Uganda, several attacks on children have happened, some of them fatal. Some of these attacks may have been due to the chimpanzees being intoxicated (from alcohol obtained from rural brewing operations) and becoming aggressive towards humans.

Human interactions with chimpanzees may be especially dangerous if the chimpanzees perceive humans as potential rivals.

[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-217)At least six cases of chimpanzees snatching and eating human babies are documented.

[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-218)

[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-219)A chimpanzee's strength and sharp teeth mean that attacks, even on adult humans, can cause severe injuries. This was evident after the attack and near death of former [NASCAR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASCAR) driver St. James Davis, who was [mauled by two escaped chimpanzees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._James_Davis_chimpanzee_attack) while he and his wife were celebrating the birthday of their former pet chimpanzee.[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-220) [218] Another example of chimpanzees being aggressive toward humans occurred in 2009 in

[Stamford, Connecticut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamford,_Connecticut), when a 90-kilogram (200 lb), 13-year-old pet chimpanzee named

[Travis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travis_(chimpanzee))attacked his owner's friend, who lost her hands, eyes, nose, and part of her

[maxilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxilla)from the attack.

[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-223)### Human immunodeficiency virus

Two primary classes of [human immunodeficiency virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_immunodeficiency_virus) (HIV) infect humans: HIV-1 and HIV-2. HIV-1 is the more virulent and easily transmitted, and is the source of the majority of HIV infections throughout the world; HIV-2 occurs mostly in west Africa. [221] Both types originated in west and central Africa, jumping from other

[primates to humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoonosis). HIV-1 has evolved from a

[simian immunodeficiency virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simian_immunodeficiency_virus)(SIVcpz) found in the subspecies

*P. t. troglodytes*of southern

[Cameroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon).


[[222]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Keele-225)

[[223]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gao-226)[Kinshasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinshasa), in the Democratic Republic of Congo, has the greatest genetic diversity of HIV-1 so far discovered, suggesting the virus has been there longer than anywhere else. HIV-2 crossed species from a different strain of HIV, found in the

[sooty mangabey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sooty_mangabey)monkeys in

[Guinea-Bissau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinea-Bissau).


[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Reeves-224)## Conservation

The chimpanzee is on the [IUCN Red List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List) as an [endangered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered) species. Chimpanzees are legally protected in most of their range and are found both in and outside [national parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_park). Between 172,700 and 299,700 individuals are thought to be living in the wild, [2] a decrease from about a million chimpanzees in the early 1900s.

Chimpanzees are listed in Appendix I of the

[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Fleur-227)[Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species)(CITES), meaning that commercial international trade in wild-sourced specimens is prohibited and all other international trade (including in parts and derivatives) is regulated by the CITES permitting system.


[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-CITES-3)The biggest threats to the chimpanzee are habitat destruction, [poaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poaching), and disease. Chimpanzee habitats have been limited by [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation) in both West and Central Africa. Road building has caused habitat degradation and fragmentation of chimpanzee populations and may allow poachers more access to areas that had not been seriously affected by humans. Although deforestation rates are low in western Central Africa, selective logging may take place outside national parks. [2] Chimpanzees are a common target for poachers. In Ivory Coast, chimpanzees make up 1–3% of

[bushmeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bushmeat)sold in urban markets. They are also taken, often illegally, for the pet trade and are hunted for medicinal purposes in some areas. Farmers sometimes kill chimpanzees that threaten their crops; others are unintentionally maimed or killed by snares meant for other animals.

Infectious diseases are a main cause of death for chimpanzees. They succumb to many diseases that afflict humans because the two species are so similar. As the human population grows, so does the risk of disease transmission between humans and chimpanzees.

## Notes

- "Great ape" is a common name rather than a taxonomic label, and there are differences in usage, even by the same author. The term may or may not include humans, as when Dawkins writes "Long before people thought in terms of evolution ... great apes were often confused with humans"
and "gibbons are faithfully monogamous, unlike the great apes which are our closer relatives".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawkins2005126-16) - One captive male, "Kermit", attained a height of 168 cm (5 ft 6 in) and a body weight of 82 kg (181 lb) when he was 11 years old.
As a fully grown adult, he weighed almost 136 kg (300 lb).[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-56)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-57) - According to A. S. Vanesyan's "Anthropology" (2015), a study by "Vorden" (probably 'Worden' or 'Warden') reported that a 54 kg (119 lb) male chimpanzee squeezed 330 kg (730 lb) on a dynamometer, while an angry female squeezed 504 kg (1,111 lb) with both hands. Of the hundreds of human students who also participated in the experiment, only one could squeeze more than 200 kg (440 lb) with both hands.
The source is said to be "Jan Dembowskiy,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-69)*The Psychology of Monkeys*".This study is listed in: Dembowski, J. (1946). "Psychology of Monkeys".[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-70)(PDF) (2nd ed.). Warsaw: Ksrazka. p. 359.
