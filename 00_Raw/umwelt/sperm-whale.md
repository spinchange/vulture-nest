### Taxonomy

The sperm whale belongs to the [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(biology)) [Cetartiodactyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetartiodactyla), [25] the order containing all

[cetaceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetaceans)and

[even-toed ungulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even-toed_ungulates). It is a member of the unranked clade

[Cetacea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetacea), with all the whales, dolphins, and porpoises, and further classified into

[Odontoceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odontoceti), containing all the toothed whales and dolphins. It is the sole extant species of its genus,

*, in the family*

[Physeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physeter)[Physeteridae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physeteridae). Two species of the related extant genus

*, the*

[Kogia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kogia)[pygmy sperm whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pygmy_sperm_whale)

*Kogia breviceps*and the

[dwarf sperm whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_sperm_whale)

*K. sima*, are placed either in this family or in the family

[Kogiidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kogiidae).

In some taxonomic schemes the families

[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-27)[Kogiidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kogiidae)and

[Physeteridae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physeteroidea)are combined as the superfamily

[Physeteroidea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physeteroidea)(see the separate entry on the

[sperm whale family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sperm_whale_family)).


[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Acrophyseter-28)Swedish ichthyologist [Peter Artedi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Artedi) described it as *Physeter catodon* in his 1738 work *Genera piscium*, from the report of a beached specimen in Orkney in 1693 and two beached in the Netherlands in 1598 and 1601. [28] The 1598 specimen was near Berkhey.


[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-30)The sperm whale is one of the species originally described by [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus) in his landmark 1758 [10th edition of Systema Naturae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae). He recognised four species in the genus

*Physeter*.

Experts soon realised that just one such species exists, although there has been debate about whether this should be named

[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-31)*P. catodon*or

*P. macrocephalus*, two of the names used by Linnaeus. Both names are still used, although most recent authors now accept

*macrocephalus*as the valid name, limiting

*catodon*'s status to a lesser synonym. Until 1974, the species was generally known as

*P. catodon*. In that year, however, Dutch zoologists Antonius M. Husson and

[Lipke Holthuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipke_Holthuis)proposed that the correct name should be

*P. macrocephalus*, the second name in the genus

*Physeter*published by Linnaeus concurrently with

*P. catodon*.

This proposition was based on the grounds that the names were synonyms published simultaneously, and, therefore, the ICZN [Principle of the First Reviser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_the_First_Reviser) should apply. In this instance, it led to the choice of *P. macrocephalus* over *P. catodon*, a view re-stated in Holthuis, 1987. [31] This has been adopted by most subsequent authors, although Schevill (1986

and 1987

[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-33)) argued that

[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-34)*macrocephalus*was published with an inaccurate description and that therefore only the species

*catodon*was valid, rendering the principle of "First Reviser" inapplicable. The most recent version of

[ITIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITIS)has altered its usage from

*P. catodon*to

*P. macrocephalus*,

following L. B. Holthuis and more recent (2008) discussions with relevant experts.

[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-35)

[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-36)Furthermore, The Taxonomy Committee of the

[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead20033-37)[Society for Marine Mammalogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society_for_Marine_Mammalogy), the largest international association of marine mammal scientists in the world, officially uses

*Physeter macrocephalus*when publishing their definitive

[list of marine mammal species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_marine_mammal_species).

### Skeleton

The ribs are bound to the spine by flexible cartilage, which allows the ribcage to collapse rather than snap under high pressure. [67] While sperm whales are well adapted to diving, repeated dives to great depths have long-term effects. Bones show the same

[avascular necrosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avascular_necrosis)that signals

[decompression sickness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decompression_sickness)in humans. Older skeletons showed the most extensive damage, whereas calves showed no damage. This damage may indicate that sperm whales are susceptible to decompression sickness, and sudden surfacing could be lethal to them.


[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-bends-69)Like that of all cetaceans, the spine of the sperm whale has reduced [zygapophysial joints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygapophysial_joint), of which the remnants are modified and are positioned higher on the vertebral dorsal spinous process, hugging it laterally, to prevent extensive lateral bending and facilitate more dorso-ventral bending. These evolutionary modifications make the spine more flexible but weaker than the spines of terrestrial vertebrates.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-70)

Like many cetaceans, the sperm whale has a vestigial pelvis that is not connected to the spine. However, recent research suggests that these bones are actually functional and are targeted by sexual selection to control reproductive muscles.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-71)

Like that of other [toothed whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothed_whale), the skull of the sperm whale is asymmetrical so as to aid [echolocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_echolocation). Sound waves that strike the whale from different directions will not be channeled in the same way. [71] Within the basin of the cranium, the openings of the bony narial tubes (from which the nasal passages spring) are skewed towards the left side of the skull.

### Biological systems

The sperm whale respiratory system has adapted to cope with drastic pressure changes when diving. The flexible [ribcage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribcage) allows lung collapse, reducing [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen) intake, and [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism) can decrease to conserve [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen).[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-85) [85] Between dives, the sperm whale surfaces to breathe for about eight minutes before diving again.


[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead_Sperm_Whale-41)[Odontoceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odontoceti)(toothed whales) breathe air at the surface through a single, S-shaped blowhole, which is extremely skewed to the left. Sperm whales spout (breathe) 3–5 times per minute at rest, increasing to 6–7 times per minute after a dive. The blow is a noisy, single stream that rises up to 2 metres (6.6 ft) or more above the surface and points forward and left at a 45° angle.

On average, females and juveniles blow every 12.5 seconds before dives, while large males blow every 17.5 seconds before dives.

#### Eyes and vision

The sperm whale's eye does not differ greatly from those of other [toothed whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothed_whale) except in size. It is the largest among the toothed whales, weighing about 170 g. It is overall ellipsoid in shape, compressed along the visual axis, measuring about 7×7×3 cm. The [cornea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornea) is elliptical and the lens is spherical. The [sclera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sclera) is very hard and thick, roughly 1 cm anteriorly and 3 cm posteriorly. There are no [ciliary muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciliary_muscle). The [choroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choroid) is very thick and contains a fibrous * tapetum lucidum*. Like other toothed whales, the sperm whale can retract and protrude its eyes, thanks to a 2-cm-thick retractor muscle attached around the eye at the equator, but are unable to roll the eyes in their sockets.


[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-127)According to Fristrup and Harbison (2002), [127]
sperm whale's eyes afford good vision and sensitivity to light. They conjectured that sperm whales use vision to hunt squid, either by detecting silhouettes from below or by detecting bioluminescence. If sperm whales detect silhouettes, Fristrup and Harbison suggested that they hunt upside down, allowing them to use the forward parts of the ventral

[visual fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_field)for

[binocular vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binocular_vision).

[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-133)## Vocalization complex

After [Valentine Worthington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Val_Worthington) and [William E. Schevill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_E._Schevill) confirmed the existence of sperm whale vocalization, [106] further studies found that sperm whales are capable of emitting sounds at a source level of 236

[decibels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decibel)– making the sperm whale the loudest animal in the world.


[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Møhl,_B.,_Wahlberg,_M.,_Madsen,_P.T.,_Heerfordt,_A._&_Lund,_A._2003_1143–1154-11)

[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Davies-13)### Mechanism

When [echolocating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_echolocation), the sperm whale emits a directionally focused beam of broadband clicks. Clicks are generated by forcing air through a pair of phonic lips (also known as "monkey lips" or "*museau de singe*") at the front end of the nose, just below the blowhole. [133] The sound then travels backwards along the length of the nose through the spermaceti organ. Most of the sound energy is then reflected off the frontal sac at the cranium and into the melon, whose lens-like structure focuses it.

Some of the sound will reflect back into the spermaceti organ and back towards the front of the whale's nose, where it will be reflected through the spermaceti organ a third time. This back and forth reflection which happens on the scale of a few milliseconds creates a multi-pulse click structure.

[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Cranford2000ImpulseSoundSources-108)

[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-135)This multi-pulse click structure allows researchers to measure the whale's spermaceti organ using only the sound of its clicks.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-136) [136] Because the interval between pulses of a sperm whale's click is related to the length of the sound producing organ, an individual whale's click is unique to that individual. However, if the whale matures and the size of the

[spermaceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermaceti)organ increases, the tone of the whale's click will also change.

The lower jaw is the primary reception path for the echoes. A continuous fat-filled canal transmits received sounds to the inner ear.

[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-JCDG_Eval_1991-137)

[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-echo-138)The source of the air forced through the phonic lips is the right nasal passage. While the left nasal passage opens to the blow hole, the right nasal passage has evolved to supply air to the phonic lips. It is thought that the nostrils of the land-based ancestor of the sperm whale migrated through evolution to their current functions, the left nostril becoming the blowhole and the right nostril becoming the phonic lips.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-tohora-139)

Air that passes through the phonic lips passes into the distal sac, then back down through the left nasal passage. This recycling of air allows the whale to continuously generate clicks for as long as it is submerged.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-140)

### Vocalization types

The sperm whale's vocalizations are all based on clicking, described in four types: the usual echolocation, creaks, codas, and slow clicks.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003135-141)

The usual echolocation click type is used in searching for prey. [140] A creak is a rapid series of high-frequency clicks that sounds somewhat like a creaky door hinge. It is typically used when homing in on prey.


[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003135-141)Slow clicks are heard only in the presence of males (it is not certain whether females occasionally make them). Males make a lot of slow clicks in breeding grounds (74% of the time), both near the surface and at depth, which suggests they are primarily mating signals. Outside breeding grounds, slow clicks are rarely heard, and usually near the surface.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003144-142)

#### Codas

The most distinctive vocalizations are codas, which are short rhythmic sequences of clicks, mostly numbering 3–12 clicks, in stereotyped patterns. [142] They are classified using variations in the number of clicks, rhythm, and tempo.


[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hersh-2022-144)Codas are the result of [vocal learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_learning) within a stable social group, [144] and are made in the context of the whales' social unit.

"The foundation of sperm whale society is the matrilineally based social unit of ten or so females and their offspring. The members of the unit travel together, suckle each others' infants, and babysit them while mothers make long deep dives to feed."

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead-2024-143)Over 70% of a sperm whale's time is spent independently foraging; codas "could help whales reunite and reaffirm their social ties in between long foraging dives".

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead-2024-143)

[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hersh-2022-144)

[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Safina-2020-pp16–19-146)While nonidentity codas are commonly used in multiple different clans, [146] some codas express clan identity, and denote different patterns of travel, foraging, and socializing or avoidance among clans.


[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Safina-2020-pp16–19-146)In particular, whales will not group with whales of another clan even though they share the same geographical area.

[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-148)Statistically, as the clans' ranges become more overlapped, the distinction in clan identity coda usage becomes more pronounced.

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead-2024-143)Distinctive codas identify seven clans described among the approximately 150,000 female sperm whales in the Pacific Ocean, and there are another four clans in the Atlantic.

[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-PNAS-20221205-147)As "arbitrary traits that function as reliable indicators of cultural group membership", clan identity codas act as symbolic markers that modulate interactions between individuals.

[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead-2024-143)

[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hersh-2022-144)Individual identity in sperm whale vocalizations is an ongoing scientific issue, however. A distinction needs to be made between cues and signals. Human acoustic tools can distinguish individual whales by analyzing micro-characteristics of their vocalizations, [148] and the whales can probably do the same. This does not prove that the whales deliberately use some vocalizations to signal individual identity in the manner of the

[signature whistles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signature_whistle)that bottlenose dolphins use as individual labels.

### Distribution

Sperm whales are among the most [cosmopolitan species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmopolitan_species). [16] They prefer ice-free waters over 1,000 metres (3,300 ft) deep.

Although both sexes range through temperate and tropical oceans and seas, only adult males populate higher

[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn-4)[latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude).

Among several regions, such as along coastal waters of southern

[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-audubon-15)[Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia), sperm whales have been considered to be locally extinct.


[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-150)They are relatively abundant from the poles to the equator and are found in all the oceans. They inhabit the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea), but not the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea), [13] while their presence in the

[Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea)is uncertain.

The shallow entrances to both the Black Sea and the Red Sea may account for their absence.

[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-iucn-4)The Black Sea's lower layers are also

### Diet

Sperm whales usually dive between 300 and 800 metres (980 and 2,620 ft), and sometimes 1 to 2 kilometres (3,300 to 6,600 ft), in search of food. [173] Such dives can last more than an hour.

They feed on several species, notably the

[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead200379-174)[giant squid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_squid), but also the

[colossal squid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colossal_squid),


[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-175)[octopuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octopus), and fish such as

[demersal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demersal_zone)

[rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batoidea)and

[sharks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shark),

[but their diet is mainly medium-sized

*]*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)[squid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squid).

Sperm whales may also possibly prey upon

[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead200343–55-176)[swordfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swordfish)on rare occasions.

Some prey may be taken accidentally while eating other items.

[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-177)Most of what is known about

[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead200343–55-176)[deep-sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep-sea)squid has been learned from specimens in captured sperm whale stomachs, although more recent studies analysed

[faeces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faeces).

One study, carried out around the [Galápagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos), found that squid from the genera * Histioteuthis* (62%),

*(16%), and*

[Ancistrocheirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancistrocheirus)*(7%) weighing between 12 and 650 grams (0.026 and 1.433 lb) were the most commonly taken.*

[Octopoteuthis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octopoteuthis)Battles between sperm whales and giant squid or colossal squid have never been observed by humans; however, white scars are believed to be caused by the large squid. One study published in 2010 collected evidence that suggests that female sperm whales may collaborate when hunting

[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-178)[Humboldt squid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt_squid).

Tagging studies have shown that sperm whales hunt upside down at the bottom of their deep dives. It is suggested that the whales can see the squid silhouetted above them against the dim surface light.

[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-179)

[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-180)An older study, examining whales captured by the New Zealand whaling fleet in the [Cook Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cook_Strait) region, found a 1.69:1 ratio of squid to fish by weight. [180] Sperm whales sometimes take

[sablefish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sablefish)and

[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-183)[megamouth shark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megamouth_shark), a rare and large deep-sea species discovered in the 1970s.

In one case, three sperm whales were observed attacking or playing with a megamouth.

Sperm whales are prodigious feeders and eat around 3% of their [body weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body_weight) per day.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-190)[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-191)

Their clicks are among the most powerful sounds in the animal kingdom ([see above](https://en.wikipedia.org#Vocalization_complex)). It has been hypothesised that it can stun prey with its clicks. [[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-LaveryEtAl2010-195)## Life cycle

Sperm whales can live 70 years or more.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-princeton-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-audubon-15)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Cetacean_Societies-16) [16] The oldest recorded living sperm whale was 77–80 years old.


[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-196)They are a prime example of a species that has been

[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Nicola–Maio-197)[K-selected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R/K_selection_theory), meaning their

[reproductive strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproductive_strategy)is associated with stable environmental conditions and comprises a low birth rate, significant parental aid to offspring, slow maturation, and high longevity.


[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead_Sperm_Whale-41)How they choose mates has not been definitively determined. Bulls will fight with each other over females, and males will mate with multiple females, making them [polygynous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygyny_in_animals), but they do not dominate the group as in a harem.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003276-198) [198] Bulls do not provide paternal care to their offspring but rather play a fatherly role to younger bulls to show dominance.


[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003343-200)Recent studies have shown that associations between groups of males and females are temporary, and that associations between groups and individual males appear to be determined by female choice rather than male aggression. As if to support this, aggression between male sperm whales is rare within breeding grounds; only one case of male-male aggression was recorded over 11 years in a 40-year study around the Galapagos Islands, and no aggression was reported in a similar 20-year study off the Dominican Republic. Males do occasionally bear teeth marks, which could have occurred during intra-sex competition, but this raises the possibility that male sperm whale teeth marks may have evolved as a signal of male dominance rather than as a direct result of competition for females.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Eguiguren-57) [16] However, observations may be rare because fights between males occur very quickly or under the water.


[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Eguiguren-57)Females appear to reach sexual maturity around 6–13 years of age. [56] Females appear to reach sexual maturity around age 9 on average and become fertile around age 10. However, the age of sexual maturity in females varies considerably across regions.


[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Eguiguren-57)Female sperm whales' reproductive capacity appears to decline steadily from the age of 10–14, then sharply after age 40.

[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003122-201)The oldest pregnant female ever recorded was 41 years old.

[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Eguiguren-57)## Social behaviour

### Relations within the species

Like elephants, females and their young live in matriarchal groups called pods, while bulls live apart. Bulls sometimes form loose bachelor groups with other males of similar age and size. As they grow older, they typically live solitary lives, only returning to the pod to socialize or to breed.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead_Sperm_Whale-41) [16] Bulls have

[beached](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetacean_stranding)themselves together, suggesting a degree of cooperation which is not yet fully understood.

The whales rarely, if ever, leave their group.

[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Whitehead_Sperm_Whale-41)

[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003232-208)A *social unit* is a group of sperm whales who live and travel together over a period of years. Individuals rarely, if ever, join or leave a social unit. There is a huge variance in the size of social units. They are most commonly between six and nine individuals in size but can have more than twenty. [208] Unlike

[orcas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_whale), sperm whales within a social unit show no significant tendency to associate with their genetic relatives.

Females and calves spend about three-quarters of their time foraging and a quarter of their time socializing. Socializing usually takes place in the afternoon.

[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003235-210)

[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead2003204-211)When sperm whales socialize, they emit complex patterns of clicks called [codas](https://en.wikipedia.org#Types_of_vocalization). They will spend much of the time rubbing against each other. Tracking of diving whales suggests that groups engage in [herding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herding) of prey, similar to [bait balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bait_ball) created by other species, though the research needs to be confirmed by tracking the prey.[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-212)[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-213)

### Relations with other species

The most common natural predator of sperm whales is the [orca (killer whale)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_whale), but [pilot whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilot_whale) and [false killer whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_killer_whale) sometimes harass them.[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-214) [214] Orcas prey on target groups of females with young, usually making an effort to extract and kill a calf. The females will protect their calves or an injured adult by encircling them. They may face inwards with their tails out (the 'marguerite formation', named after

[the flower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marguerite_daisy)). The heavy and powerful tail of an adult whale is potentially capable of delivering lethal blows.

Alternatively, they may face outwards (the 'heads-out formation'). Other than sperm whales,

[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-216)[southern right whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_right_whale)had been observed to perform similar formations.

However, formations in non-dangerous situations have been recorded as well.

[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Ponnampalam2016-217)Early whalers exploited this behaviour, attracting a whole unit by injuring one of its members.

[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Ponnampalam2016-217)Such a tactic is described in

[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-218)*:*

[Moby-Dick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moby-Dick)"Say you strike a Forty-barrel-bull—poor devil! all his comrades quit him. But strike a member of the harem school, and her companions swim around her with every token of concern, sometimes lingering so near her and so long, as themselves to fall a prey."

[[218]]

If the killer whale pod is large, its members may sometimes be able to kill adult female sperm whales and can at least injure an entire pod of sperm whales.[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-220) [220] Bulls have no predators, and are believed to be too large, powerful and aggressive to be threatened by killer whales.

Solitary bulls are known to interfere and come to the aid of vulnerable groups nearby.

[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-estes-222)However, the bull sperm whale, when accompanying pods of female sperm whales and their calves as such, may be reportedly unable to effectively dissuade killer whales from their attacks on the group, although the killer whales may end the attack sooner when a bull is present.

[[243]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-244)### Phylogeny

The traditional view has been that [Mysticeti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysticeti) (baleen whales) and [Odontoceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odontoceti) (toothed whales) arose from more primitive whales early in the [Oligocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligocene) period, and that the super-family Physeteroidea, which contains the sperm whale, dwarf sperm whale, and pygmy sperm whale, diverged from other toothed whales soon after that, over .[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FordyceBarnes2004EvolutionaryHistory-237) [238] From 1993 to 1996,

[molecular phylogenetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics)analyses by Milinkovitch and colleagues, based on comparing the

[genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genes)of various modern whales, suggested that the sperm whales are more closely related to the

[baleen whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baleen_whales)than they are to other toothed whales, which would have meant that

[Odontoceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odontoceti)were not

[monophyletic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monophyletic); in other words, it did not consist of a single ancestral toothed whale species and all its descendants.

However, more recent studies, based on various combinations of

[[234]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Nikaido-235)[comparative anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_anatomy)and molecular phylogenetics, criticised Milinkovitch's analysis on technical grounds and reaffirmed that the Odontoceti are monophyletic.

[[244]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead20032–3-245)## Usage by humans

### Sperm whaling

[Spermaceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermaceti), obtained primarily from the spermaceti organ, and [sperm oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whale_oil), obtained primarily from the [blubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blubber) in the body, were much sought after by 18th, 19th, and 20th century [whalers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whaling). These substances found a variety of commercial applications, such as [candles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candle), [soap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap), [cosmetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmetics), machine oil, other specialised lubricants, lamp oil, pencils, crayons, leather waterproofing, rust-proofing materials and many pharmaceutical compounds.[[246]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-smithsonian-247)[[247]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-highseas-248)[[248]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-249)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-aquarium-104)[Ambergris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambergris), a highly expensive, solid, waxy, flammable substance produced in the digestive system of sperm whales, was also sought as a [fixative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixative_(perfumery)) in [perfumery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfumery).[[249]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-250)

Prior to the early eighteenth century, hunting was mostly by indigenous Indonesians. [250] Legend has it that sometime in the early 18th century, around 1712, Captain Christopher Hussey, while cruising for

[right whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_whale)near shore, was blown offshore by a northerly wind, where he encountered a sperm whale pod and killed one.

Although the story may not be true, sperm whales were indeed soon exploited by American whalers. Judge

[[251]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-252)[Paul Dudley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dudley_(jurist)), in his

*Essay upon the Natural History of Whales*(1725), states that a certain Atkins, 10 or 12 years in the trade, was among the first to catch sperm whales sometime around 1720 off the

[New England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_England)coast.


[[252]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dudley-253)There were only a few recorded instances during the first few decades (1709–1730s) of offshore sperm whaling. Instead, [sloops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloop) concentrated on the [Nantucket Shoals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nantucket_Shoals), where they would have taken right whales or went to the [Davis Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis_Strait) region to catch [bowhead whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowhead_whale). By the early 1740s, with the advent of spermaceti candles (before 1743), American vessels began to focus on sperm whales. The diary of Benjamin Bangs (1721–1769) shows that, along with the bumpkin sloop he sailed, he found three other sloops flensing sperm whales off the coast of North Carolina in late May 1743. [253] On returning to Nantucket in the summer 1744 on a subsequent voyage, he noted that "45 spermacetes are brought in here this day," another indication that American sperm whaling was in full swing.


[[253]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-leviathan-254)American sperm whaling soon spread from the east coast of the American colonies to the [Gulf Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Stream), the [Grand Banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Banks), West Africa (1763), the [Azores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azores) (1765), and the South Atlantic (1770s). From 1770 to 1775 Massachusetts, New York, Connecticut, and Rhode Island ports produced 45,000 barrels of sperm oil annually, compared to 8,500 of whale oil. [254] In the same decade, the British began sperm whaling, employing American ships and personnel.

By the following decade, the French had entered the trade, also employing American expertise.

[[255]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-bockstoce-256)Sperm whaling increased until the mid-nineteenth century. Spermaceti oil was important in public lighting (for example, in lighthouses, where it was used in the United States until 1862, when it was replaced by lard oil, in turn replaced by

[[255]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-bockstoce-256)[petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum)) and for lubricating the machines (such as those used in cotton mills) of the

[Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution). Sperm whaling declined in the second half of the nineteenth century, as petroleum came into broader use. In that sense, petroleum use may be said to have protected whale populations from even greater exploitation.


[[256]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-estes2-257)Sperm whaling in the 18th century began with small sloops carrying only one or two whaleboats. The fleet's scope and size increased over time, and larger ships entered the fishery. In the late 18th century and early 19th century, sperm whaling ships sailed to the equatorial Pacific, the Indian Ocean, Japan, the coast of Arabia, Australia and New Zealand.

[[260]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-261)The sperm whale's ivory-like teeth were often sought by 18th- and 19th-century whalers, who used them to produce inked carvings known as * scrimshaw.* 30 teeth of the sperm whale can be used for ivory. [

*]*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)Modern whaling was more efficient than open-boat whaling, employing steam-powered ships and exploding [harpoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpoon). Initially, modern whaling activity focused on large [baleen whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baleen_whale), but as these populations were taken, sperm whaling increased. The hunting led to the near-extinction of large whales, including sperm whales, until bans on whale oil use were instituted in 1972. The International Whaling Commission gave the species full protection in 1985, but hunting by [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan) in the northern [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean) continued until 1988.[[257]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead200313–21-258)

From that date until 1946, the population appears to have partially recovered as whaling activity decreased, but after the Second World War, the population declined even further, to 33 per cent of the pre-whaling population.
